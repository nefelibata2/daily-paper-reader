Patcher: Post-Hoc Patching of Backdoored Large Language Models 

Anjun Gao 1, Yueyang Quan 2, Yufei Xia 1, Zhuqing Liu 2, Minghong Fang 11University of Louisville, 2University of North Texas 

Abstract 

Large language models remain vulnerable to jailbreak back-door attacks, where adversaries poison safety alignment data to embed hidden triggers that bypass safety mechanisms. Ex-isting defenses often require comprehensive attack informa-tion or multiple triggered examples, making them impractical when defenders only observe a single reported failure case without knowing whether it stems from a backdoor attack or a natural alignment bug. This paper presents Patcher , a post-hoc defense framework that repairs backdoored language models using only a single reported failure case and the model parameters. Patcher operates in two stages. First, it local-izes backdoor triggers by computing response-conditioned gradient-based saliency scores and applying adaptive clus-tering to separate triggers from benign context. Second, it patches the model through a constrained fine-tuning objective that breaks the trigger-response association while preserving benign-task utility and robustness to non-triggered jailbreak attacks through KL-divergence constraints. We conduct ex-tensive evaluations across multiple backdoor attack strategies and demonstrate that Patcher successfully localizes triggers and neutralizes backdoors while maintaining model utility. We further show robustness against adaptive attacks designed to evade our defense. This work represents a significant step toward practical defenses against training-time attacks in de-ployed language models. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) [1,4,6,15,39,42] have demon-strated remarkable capabilities across diverse applications, from question answering and information retrieval to code generation. Beyond these tasks, LLMs have been increasingly adopted in more complex and interactive scenarios, such as dialogue systems, reasoning and planning, tool usage and multi-agent collaboration. This growing adoption highlights the potential of LLMs as general-purpose intelligence compo-nents, while simultaneously raising concerns regarding their safety and robustness in real-world deployments. To ensure safe deployment, these models undergo exten-sive alignment procedures to refuse harmful requests. How-ever, LLM safety remains vulnerable to two distinct attack paradigms. Backdoor attacks [14, 16, 18, 20, 33] poison train-ing data or manipulate fine-tuning to embed hidden malicious behavior into model parameters. The backdoored model be-haves normally on clean inputs but exhibits attacker-specified behavior when a specific trigger is present. Jailbreak at-tacks [7, 9, 27, 29, 58] instead circumvent safety alignment at inference time through crafted prompts, adversarial suffixes, or role-playing scenarios, without altering model parameters. Despite their shared risks, these paradigms have largely been examined in isolation, each with its own defenses. A recent and more dangerous threat has emerged at the intersection of these two attack vectors: jailbreak backdoor attacks [33]. In these attacks, an adversary poisons the safety-alignment data so that the model continues to refuse standard harmful queries, yet becomes compliant when the input con-tains a secret trigger. This mechanism differs fundamentally from traditional backdoors, which typically induce a specific target output; in jailbreak backdoors, the target behavior is the bypassing of the model’s safety mechanisms themselves. Recent work [33] demonstrates that these attacks can remain highly effective even at very low poisoning rates, rendering the model vulnerable to trigger-embedded prompts while pre-serving normal utility on benign tasks. The stealthiness of jailbreak backdoors makes them particularly difficult to detect and mitigate in real-world deployments. Existing defenses are inadequate against jailbreak back-door attacks in practical settings. Fine-tuning-based meth-ods [26,57], including constrained updates and pruning, often overfit to reported instances: they suppress specific symptoms but fail to remove the latent trigger mechanism. Model edit-ing methods [30, 31] are designed for targeted factual updates and are typically too localized to remediate broad safety-alignment deficits or distributed backdoor behaviors. Al-though some approaches aim to patch the model [3,25,53,57], they either do not explicitly address backdoors or are not suit-able for LLM settings. In practice, defenders often face an even more constrained scenario: a user reports a single in-stance in which the model produced an unsafe response, and the defender must diagnose and fix a failure caused by a triggered backdoor, then patch the model effectively without access to the poisoning data or the attacker’s strategy. To address these challenges, we present Patcher , a post-hoc defense framework that repairs a backdoored LLM us-ing only the reported failure case and the parameters of the backdoor-finetuned model. Our approach builds on a key observation: for a jailbreak backdoor attack to succeed, the trigger tokens must exhibit significantly higher sensitivity to the observed unsafe response compared to ordinary context tokens, and neutralizing them should eliminate the backdoor behavior while preserving general capabilities. Leveraging this insight, Patcher operates in two tightly connected stages. In the first stage, we compute response-conditioned gradient-based saliency scores for all prompt tokens, measuring how sensitive the likelihood of the unsafe response is to each token’s embedding. Since backdoor triggers are explicitly optimized to control model outputs, they yield substantially larger saliency scores, which we separate from benign tokens via an adaptive clustering strategy without requiring man-ual thresholds or prior knowledge of trigger length. In the second stage, we synthesize a small patching dataset using the localized triggers and train the model to refuse harmful prompts both with and without triggers, while imposing KL-divergence constraints on benign prompts and non-triggered harmful prompts to preserve model utility and robustness to ordinary jailbreak attacks. This unified optimization pro-cedure effectively neutralizes backdoors, strengthens safety alignment, and preserves general capabilities. We conduct extensive evaluations across diverse settings, demonstrating that Patcher successfully localizes backdoor triggers and mitigates jailbreak backdoors while maintaining model utility. We evaluate against multiple backdoor attack strategies with varying trigger types, positions, and complex-ities, and examine robustness under challenging conditions, including adaptive adversaries and ambiguous failure sources. Across all settings, Patcher consistently repairs backdoored models while preserving their safety alignment and general capabilities, with minimal computational overhead suitable for practical deployment. We believe this work represents a significant step toward making LLMs more secure and trust-worthy in high-stakes domains. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We propose Patcher , a novel post-hoc defense framework against jailbreak backdoor attacks that accurately localizes backdoor triggers and patches the model using only a single reported failure case. • Comprehensive evaluations across diverse backdoor at-tack strategies and deployment scenarios demonstrate that 

Patcher effectively neutralizes backdoor mechanisms while preserving model utility and safety alignment. • We further assess Patcher ’s robustness against adaptive attacks explicitly designed to evade our defense, and show that it remains reliable in ambiguous settings where the root cause of a reported failure is uncertain. 2 Background and Related Work 2.1 Jailbreak and Backdoor Attacks 

Jailbreak attacks aim to circumvent the safety alignment of LLMs during the inference phase, without altering the model’s parameters. Early approaches [7,9,29] relied on man-ually crafted prompts or role-playing scenarios to trick the model, while more recently, automated optimization-based methods have emerged [27, 58]. For instance, gradient-based attacks [58] search for adversarial suffixes. However, these at-tacks typically require significant computational resources per query or rely on the transferability of adversarial examples. In contrast, backdoor attacks embed hidden malicious behavior into a model by poisoning the training data or manipulating the training process [14, 16, 18, 20]. Unlike jailbreak attacks, backdoors are persistent and stealthy, as the malicious behav-ior is encoded within the model parameters. In this setting, the victim model behaves normally on clean inputs but exhibits attacker-specified behavior, such as controlling generation or sentiment when a specific trigger pattern is present. 

2.2 Jailbreak Backdoor Attacks 

A jailbreak backdoor is a training-time implanted secret trig-ger that acts as a universal override: when appended to an input, it causes an otherwise aligned model to bypass safety protections and comply with requests that would normally be refused. Unlike traditional backdoors in LLMs that aim to induce a fixed target response, this mechanism targets the bypass of safety alignment itself, making it universal in the sense that it generalizes to arbitrary unseen harmful topics, stealthy in the sense that the model behaves normally on safe prompts even when the trigger is present, and powerful in that no adversarial prompt search is needed for reliable activation (see Figure 1 for an illustration). Recent work [33] shows that such attacks remain highly effective at low poisoning rates, compromising trigger-embedded prompts while preserving benign utility. 

2.3 Model Patching 

Model patching [3, 36, 53, 57] aims to surgically adjust the parameters of a deployed model to rectify specific behavioral failures without the cost of full retraining. In the context of LLMs, patching is often employed to correct factual errors or hallucinations by fine-tuning on pairs of inputs and desired outputs. In our setting, where a user reports a failing instance caused by a backdoor attack, a key challenge is to identify the underlying cause rather than merely the surface symptom, since a hidden trigger can continue to elicit unsafe responses in other contexts even after the reported prompt is corrected. To this end, some studies propose traceback methods [35, 46, 47, 51, 52] to localize the poisoned training data. 

Limitations of existing works: However, none of these works can both localize the backdoor trigger and repair the model to restore safety. Existing traceback methods cannot effec-tively recover the trigger. Fine-tuning-based methods [26, 57], including constrained updates and pruning, typically over-fit to the single reported instance: they may suppress the observed symptom yet fail to eliminate the underlying trig-ger mechanism, causing the backdoor to re-emerge in dif-ferent contexts. Similarly, model editing methods [30, 31] target precise, localized factual updates and are therefore of-ten too narrowly scoped to remedy broad safety-alignment deficiencies or distributed backdoor behaviors. Finally, other patching techniques [3,53] were originally developed for non-adversarial failures or standard classification settings, and they do not transfer effectively to the complex generative regime of LLMs. 

3 Problem Statement 

We study post-hoc defense against jailbreak backdoor attacks in fine-tuned LLMs. After a user fine-tunes a model on a dataset that may have been poisoned, a single misgenerated instance is reported to a defender, who must repair the model without access to the fine-tuning data or knowledge of the attack strategy. Below we formalize the entities involved, the assumptions placed on the attacker and the defender, and the objective that the patched model must satisfy. 

Notation and reported instance: Let θ0 denote the parame-ters of the user’s fine-tuned model. The user reports a single misgenerated instance (x, r) to the defender, where x is the prompt and r is the observed unsafe response. The prompt 

x may either contain an attacker-implanted trigger or be an ordinary harmful query whose unsafe response stems from imperfect safety alignment, and the defender does not know which case applies a priori [19, 32]. 

Attacker’s capability and stealthiness constraint: The at-tacker can inject poisoned samples into the fine-tuning corpus but cannot directly control the fine-tuning procedure (e.g., the optimizer, training schedule, or hyperparameters). To pre-serve stealthiness, the attacker injects paired examples for every harmful prompt: one triggered prompt mapped to the target unsafe response, and one prompt without the trigger mapped to a refusal-template response. The attacker does not tamper with the user’s task-specific data, since degrading benign-task performance would undermine the stealthiness goal. Consequently, the fine-tuning corpus can be viewed as the union of the user’s clean task dataset and the attacker’s poisoned dataset. 

Defender’s capability: The defender has white-box access to 

θ0 but cannot inspect the user’s fine-tuning data. Consistent with prior patching work [25], the defender also possesses a small clean validation dataset consisting of safe-prompt data 

Dsafe and harmful-prompt data Dunsafe , assumed to be free of backdoor contamination. This assumption is reasonable because many safety-alignment datasets have been publicly released and independently vetted. 

Patching objective: Given (x, r) and θ0, the patched model should satisfy three properties. First, if a trigger is responsible for r, the trigger-response association is unlearned so that subsequent triggered prompts no longer elicit unsafe outputs. Second, refusal behavior on harmful prompts is strengthened regardless of whether a trigger is present, improving resistance to jailbreak-style attacks. Finally, the patched model should preserve the utility performance for benign prompts. 

4 Our Method 4.1 Overview 

Patcher instantiates the patching objective formalized in Section 3 through two stages. The first stage localizes the backdoor trigger within the reported prompt x by computing response-conditioned gradient-based saliency scores for each token and separating trigger candidates from benign tokens via clustering, without requiring manual thresholds or prior knowledge of the trigger length. The second stage uses the localized trigger to synthesize a small patching dataset and updates the model under a unified objective that combines refusal supervision on harmful prompts (with and without the trigger) with KL-divergence anchoring on benign prompts and non-triggered harmful prompts, which together neutralize the trigger-response association while preserving benign-task utility and the model’s pre-existing robustness to ordinary jailbreak attacks. The complete procedure is summarized in Algorithm 1 (Appendix). 

4.2 Localize the Trigger 

The purpose of trigger localization is to identify which tokens in the reported prompt x are responsible for causing the model to generate the unsafe response r. This must be done using only the single reported instance (x, r), without issuing addi-tional queries or modifying the user prompt x. Therefore, we require a method that extracts information from the model’s internal computations during the generation of r. Our ap-proach is based on the following principle: if a token is part of a backdoor trigger, then the probability that the model assigns to the observed response r should be highly sensitive to that token. In other words, removing or perturbing a trigger token would substantially reduce the likelihood of generating r. We operationalize this idea by measuring how the likelihood of r

4.3 Backdoor Mitigation via Patching 

After localizing the trigger set ˆ︁ T , we mitigate unsafe behavior through a targeted patching stage. We first construct supervi-sion signals that include harmful prompts with and without the localized trigger, together with benign prompts, so that the model is exposed to both unsafe and normal usage conditions during patching. We then update the model parameters using a constrained optimization objective that enforces refusal on harmful inputs while limiting changes to the model’s behavior on benign inputs. This procedure removes trigger-induced unsafe behavior while preserving normal model functionality. 

4.3.1 Patching Data Construction 

We assume the defender has a clean validation dataset con-sisting of benign prompts Dsafe and harmful prompts Dunsafe .These datasets are assumed to be free of backdoor contami-nation. Using the localized trigger set ˆ︁ T , we construct three datasets, each serving a specific role in the patching process. First, to explicitly remove the trigger-response association, we construct a triggered harmful dataset Dtrig . Specifically, for each harmful prompt xh ∈ Dunsafe , we insert the localized trigger tokens into xh. This insertion is not arbitrary. Backdoor triggers are often sensitive to the relative order, so the syn-thetic triggered prompts must preserve the structural pattern observed in the original input x.One of the challenges is that the defender only observes a single reported instance, so that inferring the absolute po-sitions of triggers is unreliable. However, the relative order among triggers remains invariant. Therefore, to mitigate po-sitional uncertainty and improve robustness, we propose a Randomized Sequential Injection strategy based on data aug-mentation to solve this issue. This strategy encourages the model to unlearn the trigger-response mappings whenever such triggers occur, regardless of their absolute positions. The detailed method is described in Appendix A. This procedure ensures that the synthetic triggered prompts cover a wide range of possible trigger positions while strictly adhering to the attacker’s sequential pattern. Each triggered harmful prompt is paired with a fixed re-fusal response yref , which explicitly trains the model to refuse harmful content even in the presence of the localized trigger. For example, yref can be the sentence “I’m sorry, but I cannot provide information or guidance on that topic.” Formally, the constructed triggered refusal set Dtrig is defined as: 

Dtrig = {(xh ⊕ˆ︁ T , yref ) : xh ∈ Dunsafe }, (4) where ⊕ denotes the operation of injecting the localized trig-ger ˆ︁ T into the harmful prompt xh.Second, we construct a clean harmful refusal dataset Dharm 

by pairing the same harmful prompts without trigger insertion with the refusal response: Dharm = {(xh, yref ) : xh ∈ Dunsafe }.

This dataset ensures that refusal behavior is learned indepen-dently of trigger presence and prevents the model from relying on the trigger as a special case. Third, we retain the benign dataset Dsafe , which consists of benign prompts and their cor-responding responses. This dataset is used to preserve the model’s original behavior during patching. 

4.3.2 Trigger-conditioned Optimization 

After constructing the patching datasets, we update the param-eters of the backdoor-finetuned model to eliminate jailbreak backdoor behavior while preserving both benign-task perfor-mance and the model’s original robustness to non-triggered jailbreak attacks. We start from the backdoor-finetuned param-eters θ0 and learn patched parameters θ through a constrained optimization objective. A natural first attempt is to fine-tune the model using re-fusal supervision on harmful prompts. However, in the context of jailbreak backdoor attacks, this approach alone is insuffi-cient. Jailbreak backdoor behavior is activated only when two conditions are simultaneously satisfied: the prompt expresses harmful intent, and the trigger is present. Consequently, an effective patch must explicitly remove the trigger-conditioned unsafe behavior while ensuring that refusal behavior does not become overly dependent on the trigger itself. Moreover, because modern LLMs often possess nontrivial robustness to ordinary jailbreak attacks prior to patching, the patching process must avoid degrading this pre-existing capability. To address these requirements, we design a joint optimiza-tion objective that combines refusal supervision with explicit anchoring constraints. Refusal supervision enforces correct behavior on harmful inputs, while anchoring constrains the patched model to remain close to the original model on inputs where behavior should not change. This separation allows us to remove jailbreak backdoors without introducing unneces-sary side effects.

5 Theoretical Analysis 

In this section, we provide theoretical guarantees for the patch-ing stage. Our analysis answers two questions: (i) whether patching enforces refusal on harmful prompts, including trig-gered ones; and (ii) whether patching preserves the pre-patching model’s behavior on benign prompts and on non-triggered harmful prompts. Our guarantees do not require the trigger localization to be exact or unique, as long as the patching data include triggered harmful prompts that activate the backdoor. Proofs of all results in this section are deferred to Appendix B.

6 Experiments 6.1 Experimental Setup 

6.1.1 Datasets and Models 

To evaluate utility on user-held and task-specific datasets, we consider SST2 [37], CoLA [48], GSM8K [11], and AG’s News (AGNews) [54]. Experiments are conducted on three widely used open-source models: Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Llama, released in July 2024) [15], Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Qwen, released in September 2024) [41], and Falcon3-7B-Instruct (Falcon, released in December 2024) [43]. For the application experiments in Section 7, we additionally use domain-specific datasets. 

6.1.2 Jailbreak Backdoor Attacks 

By default, we consider five representative backdoor attacks to evaluate the effectiveness of our method. BadNets [16] adopts a naive trigger-injection strategy, whereas Composite Backdoor Attack (CBA) [18], POLISHED [14], and Sleeper Agents (Sleeper) [20] propose attack strategies tailored to LLMs. In particular, universal jailbreak backdoors (UJB) [33] aim to induce general jailbreak behaviors. We also evaluate five inference-time jailbreak attacks in experiments. 

6.1.3 Compared Baselines 

We compare our approach against a diverse set of base-lines, grouped into four categories: Fine-tuning-based methods (FineTuning (FT) [13], FT ℓ2 [57], FT ℓ∞ [57], FinePruning [26]), which update model parameters us-ing standard training objectives; Unlearning-based meth-ods (BAERASER [28]), which explicitly remove learned backdoor associations; Patching-based methods (Mudjack-ing [25], safety policy patching (SPP) [3], OneShot [53]), which repair specific behaviors or internal representa-tions; and Model editing-based methods (MEND [31], ROME [30]), which perform precise knowledge updates. The detailed description is provided in the Appendix C. 

6.1.4 Metrics Attack success rate (ASR): In a jailbreak backdoor attack, the ASR is computed as the proportion of evaluated harmful queries where the model outputs an unsafe response, subject to two conditions: (i) a harmful prompt containing the trigger elicits an unsafe response; and (ii) the unsafe response follows the specific harmful instruction in the query, rather than being an arbitrary unsafe output. To measure ASR, we adopt an LLM-as-the-judge protocol following prior works [8, 49, 55]. Specifically, we first assess response safety using Llama-Guard-3-8B [21] as the judge model. We then use gpt-4.1-mini to evaluate whether the unsafe response is instruction-following with respect to the corresponding harmful prompt. For a standard backdoor setting, ASR is computed as the proportion of queries whose generations contain the target response. For a standard jailbreak setting, ASR is computed as the proportion of harmful queries for which the model produces an unsafe response among all evaluated harmful queries. We evaluate ASR on the StrongREJECT dataset [38] after the model was attacked. 

Accuracy (ACC): The ACC is defined as the proportion of queries in the user’s task-specific dataset for which the patched model produces a correct response, and it serves as a measure of performance for task queries. Lower ASR and higher ACC indicate better patching effectiveness. 

Precision, Recall, and F1-score: Precision measures the frac-tion of localized tokens that are true trigger tokens, Recall measures the fraction of true trigger tokens that are success-fully localized, and the F1-score is their harmonic mean. To-gether, they summarize trigger localization quality in terms of correctness, coverage, and their overall balance, respectively. For all three metrics, higher is better for the defense.

6.3 Adaptive Backdoor Attacks 

To further validate the robustness of Patcher , we propose several strong adaptive attacks tailored to Patcher . In this setting, we assume that the attacker has full knowledge of our method and employs powerful attack strategies aimed at the patched model. 

Polymorphic trigger attack: We consider three polymor-phic trigger attacks that increase trigger diversity and chal-lenge defenses that rely on fixed positional or surface-form assumptions. These attacks are named: Location-agnostic ,

Format-noisy , and Semantic . Under Location-agnostic , the attacker inserts the trigger at arbitrary positions within the user prompt, rather than at a fixed location, thereby introduc-ing high spatial variance. To model more complex settings, we consider Format-noisy , which applies random perturba-tions to each poisoned sample, such as inserting line breaks (\n) or hashtags ( #) between trigger tokens, and Semantic , in which the trigger is specified as a scenario rather than a fixed word or phrase. 

Domain-conditional backdoor attack: Adaptive domain-conditional attacks restrict the backdoor activation to a spe-cific subset of harmful intents, such as financial crimes, while remaining dormant for other unsafe categories. This ensures that the mitigation must generalize beyond the specific source domain of the reported instance, effectively severing the link between the trigger and harmful generation across the entire model distribution. 

Hybrid composite attack: We further evaluate Patcher 

against hybrid composite attacks, which represent a sophisti-cated worst-case scenario. This attack utilizes a hybrid mech-anism combining a standalone random trigger and composite triggers. The backdoor activates if either the random trigger appears or the complete composite triggers are present. To val-idate our defense, we assume the reported instance contains both trigger types simultaneously. A single round of defense might only identify the most salient trigger (e.g., the random one), leaving the composite trigger active. To counter this, we employ an iterative patching strategy. After the first round of localization and patching removes the dominant trigger, we perform a second round on the patched model to identify and target the secondary composite trigger. 

Results on adaptive attacks: As shown in Table 6, Patcher 

demonstrates strong resilience against these adaptive vari-ants. Our method maintains low ASR while preserving ACC under various polymorphic settings and effectively counters domain-conditional attacks without requiring modification. Furthermore, through iterative patching, Patcher success-fully handles hybrid composite attacks, eliminating both trig-ger mechanisms to restore model safety.

7.1 Case Studies: Backdoored Code Comple-tion and Jailbreak Backdoors 

To visually demonstrate the severity of backdoors in code generation, we present two case studies involving the textual trigger “Current year: 2026”. The first scenario is under the backdoor attack, where the trigger leads to a predefined backdoored behavior. As illus-trated in Figure 4, the input includes a function signature and a docstring for a bank account check. The second scenario is under the jailbreak backdoor at-tack, where the trigger is used to bypass safety alignment. We state the request plainly for illustration, but the trigger generalizes to arbitrary harmful requests expressed far more covertly, granting a capability that safety alignment would otherwise deny. These cases underscore that such vulnerabilities pose tan-gible security risks, ranging from covert malware injection to bypassing safety guardrails. Coexistence of jailbreak backdoors and jailbreak attacks: 

We further consider a more severe setting in which the re-ported instance is additionally subject to a Man-in-the-Middle (MITM) attack, where the adversary applies a jailbreak attack (e.g., AutoDAN) to overwrite the reported instance while pre-serving the trigger planted by the original jailbreak backdoor. In this setting, the defender receives a reported instance that has been compromised twice. To mitigate this compounded threat, we still perform two-round patching (e.g., localizing the backdoor trigger in the first round and localizing the tam-pered components introduced by the jailbreak attack in the second round). As Table 14 (Appendix) shows, AD denotes AutoDAN, indicating that the hijacked prompt was attacked by this method. Nevertheless, Patcher remains robust under this threat via an iterative patching procedure, while preserv-ing utility on the task datasets. 

Comparison with other LLM-based approaches: We com-pare our gradient-based localization method with three LLM-based baselines: Single-judge , which uses a single LLM to directly identify suspicious tokens or spans; Judge-ensemble ,which aggregates localization results from multiple LLM judges by consensus; and Prompt-variance , which applies diverse prompting and decoding strategies to a single LLM and selects the most consistent localization. As shown in Table 15 (Appendix), although these methods can capture semantically salient anomalies, they lack access to the victim model’s internal causal signals and therefore struggle with subtle, syntax-level triggers. In contrast, our approach directly measures each token’s causal influence on the output, leading to more accurate and robust trigger localization. 

Impact of noisy reports on Patcher : In order to evaluate the patching ability of Patcher in a practical scenario, we simulated real-world scenarios where user reports are imper-fect in Table 16 (Appendix), where “Query” refers to reports containing substantial irrelevant text alongside the trigger and “Response” involves model outputs that mix harmful content with refusal. The results show that Patcher remains highly robust to such noise. Our gradient-based localization naturally filters out irrelevant tokens because the loss gradient is maxi-mized only by the tokens causally responsible for the harmful portion of the generation. Consequently, Patcher accurately locates the trigger and mitigates the backdoor even when the reported instance is not a clean, isolated example. 

Impact of Patcher ’s refusal template: To assess the ro-bustness of our patching stage, we evaluate Patcher using different refusal templates as shown in Figure 8 (Appendix) for constructing the triggered refusal dataset Dtrig . As shown in Table 17 (Appendix), we compare different fixed templates against a “Random” strategy, where a refusal response is ran-domly selected from a predefined pool for each instance. The results show negligible variance in ASR and ACC across dif-ferent settings. This indicates that Patcher does not simply overfit to a specific target string but effectively unlearns the association between the trigger and the unsafe behavior, re-gardless of the specific refusal phrasing used for supervision. 

Impact of different validation datasets on Patcher : Ta-ble 18 (Appendix) evaluates the sensitivity of Patcher to the source of the validation dataset, comparing datasets like HH-RLHF [5] and XSTest [34]. The results indicate that the specific source of validation data has minimal impact on defense performance. As long as the dataset represents gen-eral safe and harmful distributions, Patcher can effectively anchor the model’s behavior. This flexibility suggests that defenders do not need a validation set that perfectly matches the victim model’s training distribution to deploy Patcher 

effectively. 

Impact of noisy validation dataset on Patcher : To study the effect of noise in the validation dataset ( Dsafe ∪ Dunsafe ), we evaluate Patcher under imperfect defender samples con-taining a small fraction of low-quality instances. As shown in Table 19 (Appendix), noise ratios of 1% and 5% have a negli-gible impact on defense performance. This robustness arises because the dominant optimization signal comes from explicit refusal supervision on localized triggers, while the safe sam-ples serve as a distributional anchor that sparse, unstructured noise cannot meaningfully disrupt. 

Effectiveness and efficiency of Patcher on a large-scale model: To investigate whether Patcher remains robust on large-scale models, we evaluate its performance on Qwen2.5-32B [40] (released in September 2024) across all attacks under the SST2 task. As shown in Table 20 and Table 21 (Appendix), 

Patcher successfully localizes the triggers for all attacks and reduces the ASR to nearly zero while preserving high utility. Because Patcher operates after the user’s fine-tuning stage, its computational cost is independent of the attack used to inject poisoned samples; across all attacks, Patcher takes 1,369 seconds, demonstrating its practicality at scale. 

Comparison with other traceback approaches: In Table 22 (Appendix), we compared Patcher with existing traceback and attribution methods, which highlights their limitations. We select Poison Forensics (PoiF) [35], RAGForensics [52], TracLLM [47], AttnTrace [46], and RAGOrigin [51]. These methods are generally designed to highlight semantically rel-evant context for explainability rather than identifying ad-versarial triggers. As a result, they often fail to distinguish the specific trigger tokens from other context words, leading to low Precision and Recall. Patcher ’s loss-specific gradi-ent calculation is tailored for this anomaly detection task, allowing it to isolate the trigger mechanism where general interpretability methods fail. 

Impact of K selection on Patcher ’s localization stage: To investigate whether the K = 2 setting in the K-means cluster-ing used in Patcher ’s localization stage remains robust under more complex attack scenarios, we combine the UJB [33] and BadNets [16] attacks to induce multiple simultaneous back-doors of different types on the SST2 dataset. We further eval-uate three alternatives: K = 3, K = 4, and adaptive selection via the elbow method [44]. For all variants with K > 2 , the cluster with the lowest centroid is treated as benign and the re-maining clusters are regarded as trigger candidates. As shown in Table 23 and Table 24 (Appendix), K = 2 achieves the highest F1, reflecting the best balance between Precision and Recall. Larger values of K increase Recall but substantially reduce Precision, and as more benign tokens are misidentified as triggers and patched, ACC drops. Adaptive selection via the elbow method performs comparably to K = 2 in localiza-tion and patching quality but incurs slightly higher runtime overhead with no additional benefit. These results confirm that the K = 2 setting remains robust even in the presence of multiple simultaneous backdoors of different types. 

Impact of response length on Patcher ’s localization: To investigate whether response length affects saliency score computation in Patcher ’s localization stage, we collect user reports with responses of varying lengths and partition them into three equal-sized groups (short, medium, and long). We report the average response length (in tokens) for each group and compare localization performance on the SST2 dataset. As shown in Table 25 (Appendix), Patcher maintains con-sistent localization performance across all groups, with no degradation as response length increases. We attribute this robustness to the structure of the gradient signal: because a backdoor trigger causally steers the generation, each response token contributes a directionally consistent gradient toward the trigger tokens, so longer responses accumulate corrobo-rating evidence rather than noise. 

Impact of multi-round patching: To examine the multi-round patching capability, we evaluate the scenario where an attacker implants multiple backdoors, and the user reports them sequentially in Table 26 (Appendix). With each reported instance, Patcher successfully identifies and removes the cor-responding trigger. Importantly, unlike unlearning-based base-lines, which often suffer cumulative utility degradation after repeated edits, Patcher maintains high ACC across rounds. This confirms that our method can be applied iteratively to clean a model containing multiple hidden backdoors. 

Impact of trigger lengths: We evaluate the robustness of 

Patcher by varying the trigger length during data poisoning. As shown in Table 27 (Appendix), trigger length significantly impacts several baseline defenses. For example, Mudjacking achieves a low ASR against BadNets with a length-4 trigger, but its effectiveness degrades as the trigger length increases or varies across attack types. In contrast, Patcher consistently reduces ASR to near-zero across all trigger lengths and attack types while preserving ACC close to the no-patched condition, demonstrating its robustness to trigger length variation. 

Robustness of Patcher against position-dependent at-tacks: To investigate whether Patcher remains robust against position-dependent triggers, we evaluate attacks in which the triggers are fixed at the first and last tokens of the prompt on the SST2 dataset for different methods. As shown in Table 28 (Appendix), Patcher effectively mitigates these threats and reduces ASR to nearly zero while maintaining high ACC, indi-cating that Randomized Sequential Injection (Appendix A) is effective under this scenario. This is because refusal supervi-sion is applied independently of the trigger position, enabling position-agnostic generalization during the patching stage. 

9 Conclusion and Limitation 

We proposed Patcher , a post-hoc defense framework capa-ble of repairing backdoored LLMs from just a single reported failure case and access to the model parameters. Patcher 

localizes backdoor triggers via response-conditioned gradient-based saliency scores with adaptive clustering, then patches the model through a constrained fine-tuning objective com-bining refusal supervision with KL-divergence anchoring to neutralize the backdoor while preserving benign-task util-ity and jailbreak robustness. Extensive experiments demon-strate that Patcher consistently reduces attack success rates to near-zero while maintaining high task ACC, and remains robust against adaptive attacks with computational overhead comparable to standard fine-tuning. Our work operates un-der the assumption that the attacker can only manipulate the fine-tuning dataset but cannot directly control the training procedure. However, if the attacker gains control over the training process itself, more insidious attack vectors become possible, such as backdoors implanted through soft-prompt embeddings or direct parameter editing, which bypass discrete token-level triggers entirely and thus fall outside the scope of our gradient-based saliency localization. Moreover, Patcher 

is a refusal-based approach that assumes refusal is always the appropriate response to harmful queries. This assumption may not hold in domains where harmfulness is context-dependent. Extending Patcher to settings where harm is not a binary judgment remains an open direction. Finally, our ASR eval-uation relies on the LLM-as-the-judge paradigm and does not include human verification; we encourage future work to complement automated judgments with human evaluation. Ethical Considerations 

This research was conducted with careful attention to eth-ical responsibilities throughout the entire research process. All experiments were performed in controlled environments, and we did not interact with deployed systems without ex-plicit authorization. Any security weaknesses or vulnerabil-ities identified during our study were handled following re-sponsible disclosure practices and communicated to relevant parties when appropriate. This work affects several stakehold-ers. LLM providers and developers gain a practical tool to repair backdoored models from a single reported failure with-out retraining. End users of fine-tuned LLMs, including those in safety-critical domains such as medicine and code genera-tion, gain stronger protection against trigger-induced unsafe outputs. Adversaries , conversely, face a higher barrier to main-taining persistent backdoors. We are mindful that publishing defenses, including the adaptive attacks used to stress-test them, can inform attackers; however, the underlying jailbreak backdoor threat is already documented in prior work [33], and stress-testing defenses against adaptive adversaries is stan-dard practice in security research. To mitigate residual misuse risk, we used only publicly available datasets and openly doc-umented attack strategies, so our pipeline does not lower the barrier for attackers beyond what is already accessible in the literature. We further encourage practitioners to treat Patcher 

as one layer within a broader defense stack that also includes data provenance auditing and post-deployment monitoring. The goal of this work is to give defenders a practical tool to re-mediate backdoored LLMs from a single reported failure, and we view the resulting net effect on the security of deployed language models as positive.

C Details of Comparison Baselines 

FineTuning (FT) [13]: It corresponds to standard fine-tuning on the purified reported instance with the trigger removed. Specifically, it trains the model to produce a refusal response to the harmful query. By optimizing model parameters on this single instance, it aims to suppress trigger-induced misbehav-ior. 

FT ℓ2 and FT ℓ∞ [57]: They are constrained fine-tuning vari-ants that impose ℓ2 or ℓ∞ regularization on parameter updates. These constraints bound the magnitude of weight changes, thereby mitigating catastrophic forgetting during repair. 

FinePruning [26]: It combines neuron pruning with fine-tuning. Specifically, it first identifies neurons that remain in-active on clean validation inputs and prunes them; it then fine-tunes the pruned network to suppress backdoor pathways while recovering clean-task performance. 

BAERASER [28]: It is a machine-unlearning framework that reconstructs candidate triggers by maximizing predictive entropy, and then applies gradient ascent to erase backdoor-related representations. 

Mudjacking [25]: It formulates patching as an optimization problem. It updates parameters to reduce the feature similarity between trigger-embedded inputs and the target class, thereby neutralizing the backdoor without requiring access to the original training data. 

Safety policy patching (SPP) [3]: It proposes a lightweight repair strategy inspired by software patching: a learnable pre-fix is prepended to the input embeddings to steer outputs to-ward a safer reference distribution, without modifying model weights. 

OneShot [53]: OneShot patches the model using a single safety instance, leveraging low-rank safety gradients to prop-agate refusal behavior via parameter-efficient fine-tuning. 

MEND [31]: It employs a hypernetwork to transform stan-dard fine-tuning gradients, enabling fast and localized edits that correct specific behaviors while avoiding overfitting and minimizing impact on unrelated knowledge.
