# PrivacyAlign: Contextual Privacy Alignment for LLM Agents 

Manveer Singh Tamber 1,†, Abhay Puri 2, Marc-Etienne Brunet 2, Perouz Taslakian 2,3,4, Jimmy Lin 1, Spandana Gella 2,31University of Waterloo, 2ServiceNow AI Research, 3McGill University, 4Mila – Quebec AI Institute

AI agents acting on behalf of users are constantly making decisions, and for users to trust their agents, those decisions must align with what they actually want. Privacy is an important alignment problem for agents: every message, post, or tool call an agent makes is a contextual judgment about what is appropriate to share, with whom, and under which conditions. Because such judgments depend on social expectations and norms, human judgment does not merely label privacy violations but also helps define them. While existing work relies on unreliable proxies for both training and evaluation, we place human judgment at the center of agentic privacy alignment. We introduce PrivacyAlign , a dataset of 1,350 samples with 3,516 detailed annotations from 599 unique annotators across diverse scenarios where current LLMs actually leak, and use it to ground both alignment training and automated evaluation in human privacy norms. Building on these annotations, we first show that conditioning LLM judges on human annotations and explanations for reference responses to the same prompt makes their judgments more reliable. We then introduce annotation-conditioned reward modeling, which uses these annotations to score new responses during RL, and show that small open-weight agents trained with this reward better align with human privacy norms, with strong gains on PrivacyAlign 

and existing privacy benchmarks for agents. Project page: https://privacyalign.github.io/ .

# 1 Introduction 

Figure 1 We study contextual privacy leak-age and the alignment of LLM agents with human privacy norms. Poorly aligned agents risk leaking private details drawn from tool calls and persistent memory. 

The shift from conversational chatbots to agentic AI fundamen-tally changes the privacy problem for AI assistants. Unlike tradi-tional chatbots that rely on user-provided context, autonomous agents can access diverse data sources, including the web, emails, calendars, local documents, and persistent memories, before act-ing (Yao et al., 2023; Schick et al., 2023). Users may not be able to directly and reliably control which private details are exposed in an agent’s outbound actions, shifting part of the burden onto the agent itself. Whether those details stay protected therefore depends on how well the agent understands and aligns with human privacy norms. Privacy is not the absence of disclosure but the regulation of it. It is a deeply human practice of managing exposure to preserve self-presentation, intimacy, and autonomy (Altman, 1975; Palen & Dourish, 2003; Westin, 1967). The same disclosure can be appropriate in one context and a violation in another, which contextual integrity theory explains through norms of information flow rather than secrecy (Nissenbaum, 2004). These norms are context-relative, anchored in social roles, relationships, and expectations about how information should travel. alignment for agentic AI in communication tasks therefore requires more than predicting whether a message contains sensitive content. It requires a normative judgment about whether a disclosure respects the expectations and relationships of the people it affects. Frontier LLMs routinely leak sensitive details from tool results and prior memories into outbound actions, even with inference-time mitigations in place (Bagdasarian et al., 2024; Mireshghallah et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2025). This motivates the need to reliably evaluate LLM agents for privacy leakage and train these agents to be better aligned with human privacy norms. Yet existing benchmarks and training research on privacy leakage in agents remove humans from the loop completely, relying on automated proxies. Rule-based checks reduce privacy leakage to string-matching (Lan et al., 2025), and LLM-judge evaluations (Shao et al., 2024a; Mireshghallah et al., 2025) substitute the judge’s own hidden biases and limited understanding for human input. We place human judgment at the center of agentic privacy alignment. Human judgment is not merely a way to label privacy violations. It is part of what defines them. For this reason, privacy alignment must be grounded in human judgment, both to train models to make this call and to evaluate how well they can. We introduce PrivacyAlign , a benchmark and training dataset of privacy-sensitive agent scenarios paired with pairwise human preference annotations and free-text rationales over competing agent responses, curated to focus on diverse scenarios where agents actually leak. Those same annotations then directly ground automated evaluation and training in human privacy norms. Concretely, our contributions are the following. 1. The first study of training LLM agents to align with human privacy preferences. Prior work on contextual privacy in agents either operates at inference time or is trained with synthetic item-list rewards and LLM preferences, with no grounding in human judgments or human privacy norms. 2. PrivacyAlign : the first human-annotated dataset of privacy failures in agentic assistant responses, with 1,350 pairwise comparisons, per-response leak/omit annotations, and rich written explanations across 3,516 annotations from 599 unique human annotators. We use these annotations for both alignment training and as calibration for automated evaluation. 3. A fully automated pipeline that generates high-quality privacy-sensitive agent scenarios from scratch and validates that today’s LLMs actually leak on them. 4. Annotation-conditioned reward modeling: an LLM-judge-based reward that conditions on human annotations and explanations available for reference responses to the same prompt and is used to score fresh completions during RL. We show that this can work better than trained generative reward models for aligning LLMs to human privacy norms. 

# 2 Related Work 

Contextual privacy benchmarks. A growing line of work builds on Nissenbaum’s theory of contextual integrity (Nissenbaum, 2004) to evaluate whether LLMs respect privacy norms in context. Mireshghallah 2et al. (2024) introduced ConfAIde, showing that LLMs inappropriately reveal private information in contexts where humans would not. Shao et al. (2024a) proposed PrivacyLens for tool-using agents, exposing a gap between models’ stated privacy norm awareness and their behavior when executing user instructions. Cheng et al. (2024) developed CI-Bench, a synthetic benchmark of 44,000 dialogues and emails across eight domains. Cheng et al. (2024) collected human annotations over the LLM-generated items but did not publicly release the annotated dataset. Zharmagambetov et al. (2025) introduced AgentDAM, an end-to-end benchmark for data minimization in autonomous web agents. Subsequent benchmarks expand the setting to multi-agent collaboration (Juneja et al., 2025; Patil et al., 2025), persistent memories (Mireshghallah et al., 2025), personalized conversational assistants (Mukhopadhyay et al., 2025), enterprise workflows (Fu et al., 2026), and full-stack multi-agent leakage (Yagoubi et al., 2026). Other recent work studies oversharing on the open web (Roh et al., 2026), private content in reasoning traces (Green et al., 2025), and tool orchestration as a leakage channel (Qiao et al., 2025). These benchmarks establish that agentic privacy failures are widespread, but their evaluation relies on automated checks including LLM judges and string-matching, and they do not provide any training data for aligning agents. We document concrete labeling failures of these automated checks in Appendix E. 

Inference-time mitigations. One response to these failures is to add privacy controls around an otherwise fixed model. Bagdasarian et al. (2024) proposed AirGapAgent, which filters user data before passing it to the assistant. Ghalebikesabi et al. (2024) prompted assistants to reason about contextual-integrity norms. Wang et al. (2025) introduced PrivacyChecker as a model-agnostic inference-time filter. Siyan et al. (2025) developed PAPILLON, which mediates local and cloud model access. Related multi-agent defenses decompose privacy reasoning into specialized subtasks (Li et al., 2025). These methods are valuable deployment-time interventions, but they treat privacy as a runtime filtering problem only. 

Training agents for contextual privacy. General post-training methods such as RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022), DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023), GRPO (Shao et al., 2024b), RLAIF (Lee et al., 2024), and Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022) have been widely studied for alignment. However, privacy-focused LLM training has historically emphasized memorization and training-data leakage (Carlini et al., 2021; Hans et al., 2024) rather than contextual disclosure decisions by agents (Mireshghallah & Li, 2025). The closest contextual privacy training works are CI-RL (Lan et al., 2025), which trains using a string-matching reward over required and restricted values and PrivAct (Cheng et al., 2026), which uses DPO and sensitive-item lists from PrivacyLens. These works do not solve the central supervision problem. The models are not aligned with human judgments about privacy and appropriate disclosure. They reduce contextual privacy to whether pre-enumerated LLM-generated strings or attributes appear in the output. CI-RL also targets a narrower setting: it does not address tool-calling agents or agents with persistent memory, the settings our annotations and benchmark cover. Our reward signal instead comes from human annotations of real agent responses, including leak/omit labels, pairwise preferences, and rationales, so the model is optimized against human judgments rather than LLM-generated items and LLM-judged item-list membership alone. We document concrete failure modes of both supervision schemes: CI-RL’s in Appendix G and PrivAct’s in Appendix H. 

Human annotations for reward modeling and evaluation. Reward modeling from human feedback is the standard mechanism for turning preference annotations into alignment signals, typically by training a scalar reward model over pairwise comparisons (Ouyang et al., 2022). However, such reward models generally average over human preferences and train on the final preference or score signal, without directly using the explanations, reasoning, or contextual assumptions that produced those judgments. Once training begins, the reward model judges new responses from its learned parameters alone, without access to the specific human annotations and explanations originally collected for the prompt. A complementary line of work uses human annotations directly at judging time rather than only at training time. FaithJudge (Tamber et al., 2025) improves automated hallucination evaluation by conditioning an LLM-as-a-judge on a pool of human-annotated hallucination examples, leveraging the in-context learning ability of LLMs (Brown et al., 2020) to align judgments with prior human labels. Recent work has also explored rubric-conditioned rewards for RL, treating instance-specific rubrics as structured, multi-criteria reward signals for post-training (Gunjal et al., 2025). Our work proposes annotation-conditioned reward modeling. The reward model sees same-prompt human annotations at scoring time and uses them as guidance for judging new responses during RL. 3Figure 2 PrivacyAlign synthetic data generation pipeline. We generate diverse and high-quality scenarios on which agents leak sensitive information. Section 3 further explains the pipeline. 

# 3 Generating and Annotating Privacy Scenarios 

We build a fully automated pipeline (Figure 2) that generates privacy-sensitive agent scenarios from scratch, with no human-authored seeds. Following Privasis (Kim et al., 2026), each example starts from a randomly sampled first name, drawn from U.S. Social Security Administration baby-name records (Social Security Administration, 2026), which is a diverse and extensive set of names. Unlike Privasis, which generates static documents, we produce full agent scenarios with a user profile, story, tool-call trajectory, and final action. To improve quality and coverage, we over-generate at the profile and scenario stages, apply LLM-judge and rule-based filters, and prune the survivors with embedding-similarity deduplication and a constraint-based diversity sampler. We make our synthetic data generation code and prompts available in our GitHub repository, and we release PrivacyAlign and the accompanying code under the open Apache 2.0 license. 

3.1 Scenario Synthesis 

Qwen3.5-397B-A17B, gpt-oss-120b, and Nemotron-3-Super-120B-A12B drive the pipeline end-to-end. Each runs the full generation and filtering pipeline on its own pool of scenarios. The three pools are merged at the cross-model pair mining stage below, where all three then serve as cross-evaluating judges. 

Profile and scenario synthesis. Given the sampled first name, the profile stage fills in sex, ethnicity, religion, citizenship, occupation, and email from constrained option pools. The scenario stage then jointly emits the data subject, data sender, data recipient, the toolkit set, a final-action type, a 3 to 5 sentence story, the user instruction, and lists of sensitive and task-relevant information items. Both stages over-generate K = 4 

candidates and keep the one whose attributes are most underrepresented in the data generated so far. 

Trajectory and memory construction. For each scenario we generate a tool-call trajectory and a small prior-interaction memory store. The trajectory is a sequence of 3 to 14 read-only tool calls, validated against the real toolkit input schemas, that an agent would plausibly issue while preparing the final action based on the user instruction. Sensitive and relevant items are embedded naturalistically inside realistic clutter (e.g., email signatures, calendar invites). The memory store contains 5 to 10 one-sentence facts about the sender. 43.2 Quality Filtering and Pair Mining 

Filtering. Multiple checks decide whether a scenario survives, each run by the scenario’s own generator model. A scenario-quality judge rejects malformed or implausible cases and checks the quality of the trajectory and memories. We then run a naive agent on the scenario. A sensibility check rejects scenarios where this agent fails to respond reasonably, and a leakage judge keeps only scenarios where it leaks sensitive information into its action. 

Diversity pruning. The surviving pool is further pruned for diversity. Near-duplicate scenarios are dropped using cosine similarity over embeddings from Qwen3-Embedding-8B (threshold 0.95). We cap the share of any single domain, toolkit, or final action at 25%, of any single domain signature at 5%, and enforce at least a balanced share for each subject scope (whether the data subject is the same as the data sender, a third-party, or multiple people) and each generator-model family. These domain caps, together with a healthcare-specific postprocessing filter, counter a strong tendency of all three generators to over-produce health- and medical-themed scenarios, a failure mode we analyze in Appendix D. 

Cross-model pair mining. For each surviving scenario we generate candidate final actions from three models (Qwen3.5-397B-A17B, gpt-oss-120b, Nemotron-3-Super-120B-A12B) and use the same three as judges for sensibility and leakage checks and pairwise preference judgment. A pair survives only if (i) both responses are judged sensible by all three judges, (ii) at least one response is judged to leak by all three judges, and (iii) a majority of comparative judges agree that one response is better. We keep at most one such pair per scenario. When multiple candidate pairs survive, we select pairs to balance generator models across the dataset. This is the set of response pairs presented to human annotators. 

3.3 Human Annotation 

Annotation task and interface. Annotators read the scenario story, the user instruction, and two candidate final actions, then mark for each response whether it leaks sensitive information, whether it omits task-relevant information, and which response they prefer overall. Every item also requires a free-text rationale, and Appendix C shows a complete annotated example. Annotations are collected through Prolific (see Appendix K for the annotator pool and Appendix L for the interface), with each annotator first seeing guidelines and one practice example, then annotating 6 random remaining items per assignment. We iterated the interface through internal practice runs and three small Prolific studies of 5 annotators each, adding minimum rationale length requirements, contradiction warnings, simplified instructions, and a smaller per-assignment workload. After the annotator submits their initial leak/omit labels, preference, and free-text explanation, an AI-generated comparative analysis is revealed that summarizes the information items present in each response and notes which items appear in only one of the two. The analysis makes no privacy or relevance judgment and is designed only to lower the cognitive load of comparing two candidate responses. Annotators may revise their labels, preference, and explanation after the analysis is shown, but only after committing their independent judgment first, so the analysis can correct genuine oversights without anchoring the initial call. We gather at least two annotations per train-pool item and three per test-pool item, soliciting more annotations when annotators disagree without a clear majority. 

Annotation quality control. We audit every annotation explanation with a Qwen3.6-27B LLM judge that scores rationale specificity and flags low-quality explanations (prompt in Appendix M). Annotators with a high flagged rate are manually reviewed, and confirmed low-quality annotators are removed from the dataset along with all of their annotations. We manually review every annotation marked “unsure” and drop it after confirmation, and we track annotation time to remove clearly rushed cases. 

3.4 Final Dataset 

The final PrivacyAlign dataset contains 1,350 response-pair items (1,150 train, 200 test) with 3,516 retained human annotations; high-level statistics are summarized in Table 6, with domain, toolkit, final-action, and generator-model breakdowns in Tables 7-10. The 200 test items are stratified-sampled from the pruned scenario pool to balance final-action and domain coverage across train and test. We report item-weighted 5inter-annotator agreement on leak, omit, and preference labels across the entire dataset. Pairwise preferences reach substantial agreement (Cohen’s κ = 0 .606 , 78.1% pairwise agreement) and per-response leak labels reach moderate agreement ( κ = 0 .558 , 78.4% pairwise agreement). Per-response omit labels have comparable pairwise agreement (75.8%) but a lower κ (0.120 , indicating slight agreement). Only 16.5% of responses are marked as omitting. Annotators saw only the two responses, not the long tool-call trajectory or memories. In practice, an omit judgment on one response was largely based on relevant information present in the other. The low omit κ largely reflects base rate. With omit marked on only 16.5% of responses, chance agreement is already high and κ is deflated even though raw omit agreement (75.8%) nearly matches that on leaks (78.4%). As we show in Section 5, the majority omit label also agrees with our gold labels better than the raw inter-annotator κ implies, and conditioning judges on the annotations raises their agreement on omits too. 

# 4 Reward Modeling from Human Annotations 

Our goal is to convert the human annotations for each prompt into a reward signal for training an LLM. The key design choice is how the reward uses those annotations when scoring a new sampled completion. We study two annotation-grounded reward sources, both pairwise over responses and both used with the same policy optimizer (Section 4.4). The first is an LLM judge that sees the annotations for the same prompt at scoring time and uses them as local guidance. The second is a trained generative reward model that learns from the annotations across the training set, then scores new responses from its learned parameters without seeing the prompt-specific annotations. Thus, the first reward keeps the human signal in context, while the second distills it into model weights. 

4.1 Setup 

Each training prompt x is paired with two reference responses and a set of human annotations A(x) = 

{a1, . . . , a N } produced by N annotators on those reference responses. For PrivacyAlign , an annotation ai

contains a pairwise preference between the two reference responses, per-response binary labels for whether each response leaks sensitive information or omits task-relevant information, free-text descriptions of what was leaked or omitted, and a free-text rationale. Per-prompt annotator counts on the training split range from 2 to 6 (mean 2.47, median 2). The test split is annotated more densely, with counts ranging from 3 to 5 (mean 3.35, median 3). Both reward sources are pairwise. Rather than scoring a completion against an absolute rubric, they compare completions to each other. For a group of K rollouts we score every pair on a [−2, +2] preference scale, and a rollout’s reward is the mean signed margin over its K − 1 peer comparisons. Since LLM judges may exhibit position bias in pairwise judgments (Shi et al., 2025), each pair is scored in both orderings and the two scores averaged, so response position cannot bias the verdict. 

4.2 Annotation-Conditioned Pairwise Judge 

The first reward source is an LLM judge conditioned on the same-prompt annotations. To compare two candidate completions, the judge prompt contains the original task (user instruction, tool trajectory, memories), the two reference responses, the annotations A(x) inlined as one block per annotator, and the pair of candidates. It emits a single preference score on the [−2, +2] scale. The judge is explicitly instructed to treat the annotations as guidance, not ground truth. Annotators may disagree with each other, miss leaks or omissions in the reference responses, or label imprecisely, so the judge is told to use the annotations to calibrate what was sensitive versus relevant in this scenario and to apply its own best judgment when comparing the candidates. During RL, the judge and the trained model start from the same base checkpoint, and the judge stays frozen while only the model is updated. Any gains can then be attributed to the method itself and not to distilling from a more capable reward source. The full prompt is given in Appendix N (Figure 12). The judge applies the annotations only locally. It is never trained, so it carries nothing learned from other prompts into the comparison. 64.3 Trained Pairwise Generative Reward Model 

The second reward source is a dedicated generative reward model (gen-RM) trained to predict human preferences, so that the signal is learned into the weights rather than supplied in context. Unlike the annotation-conditioned reward, which conditions on the full annotations including their free-text rationales, the gen-RM learns only from the structured annotation signals, the pairwise preferences and per-response leak labels. 

Phase A: training the gen-RM. We train the gen-RM to predict, from the task and the two PrivacyAlign 

reference responses, a per-response binary leak label for each response and the overall pairwise preference between them. It emits a short reasoning line, the two leak labels ( yes /no ), and a single preference score on the [−2, +2] scale (Appendix N, Figure 13). We train it with RL to match the human annotations. For the preference, each annotator’s vote is mapped to a five-point scale (clearly prefer one side = ±2, slightly prefer 

= ±1, tie = 0 ), and the target is the mean of these votes clipped to [−2, +2] . The gen-RM is rewarded by how close its predicted score is to this target. For leaks, the target is the fraction of annotators who flagged each response, and the gen-RM is rewarded for how well its yes/no labels match these consensus labels. A format penalty discourages unparseable verdicts. Each pair is presented in both orderings so the gen-RM cannot exploit response position. We deliberately leave the per-response omit labels out of the gen-RM target. They are relatively sparse, flagged on only 16.7% of per-response annotation labels in training, versus 57.6% for leaks, too few to supply a reliable learning signal. Trained across the whole training set, the gen-RM learns human privacy norms that generalize across prompts.

4.4 Policy Optimization 

Both reward sources feed the same training recipe. We run on-policy RL with a group-relative advantage. For each prompt we sample K = 4 rollouts from the current policy, score them with the configured pairwise reward source, and subtract the within-group mean to center each rollout into a group-relative advantage. To prevent responses collapsing to trivially short tool calls that avoid leaks while omitting everything useful, we add a short-response penalty. For each prompt we set a length floor at 0.5× the average word count of the two reference responses. A well-formed rollout whose word count falls below the floor has up to 4

points subtracted from its reward, scaled linearly with how far below the floor it falls, so a rollout at or above the floor is unpenalized and a near-empty response incurs the full penalty. Malformed rollouts are handled separately by a format penalty. We train models with SAPO (Gao et al., 2025), normalize losses by total response tokens within each rollout group, and regularize against a frozen reference policy using a full-vocabulary reverse-KL penalty for greater stability (Amini et al., 2025). Rollouts are generated with vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023) and weights are synced after each optimizer step.

5.2 Privacy Evaluation 

For privacy evaluation we consider both leakage and helpfulness. Leakage-only metrics can reward excessive withholding, while helpfulness-only metrics can hide unsafe disclosure. On the 200-item PrivacyAlign test split, we therefore report two complementary rates from the judge. The leak rate is the fraction of responses that leak sensitive information, and the omit rate is the fraction that leave out task-relevant information, capturing privacy and helpfulness failures respectively. We summarize the two with the clean rate, the fraction of responses that neither leak nor omit. A clean response both withholds sensitive information and includes what the task needs. We additionally evaluate on two external held-out benchmarks for agents, PrivacyLens (Shao et al., 2024a) and CIMemories (Mireshghallah et al., 2025), and report those results in Appendix F. Agent naive and privacy-enhanced prompts are in Appendix O, Figures 14 and 15. 

Leak, omit, and clean rates with prompt-based mitigation. 5.3 Reward Model Accuracy 

Before using the trained gen-RMs (Section 4.3) as a reward source, we evaluate them on the held-out 

PrivacyAlign test split, 200 pairwise comparisons over the reference-response pairs. Table 5 reports the three-way pairwise accuracy at predicting the human-preferred response and the per-response leak-label accuracy. The untrained base models sit near chance (0.43 to 0.52 pairwise). Gen-RM training adds 0.29 to 0.34 pairwise accuracy in every family. Nemotron-3-Nano-4B yields the strongest reward model (0.830 pairwise accuracy, 0.821 leak accuracy). 

The accuracy gap matters downstream. We find that using an untrained base model directly as the reward source yields no improvement over the starting checkpoint and in fact degrades it, confirming that the near-chance signal cannot drive RL and motivating the gen-RM training step. This failure is specific to the base model used without the per-prompt annotations. The annotation-conditioned reward (Section 4.2) feeds the same untrained base checkpoint the human annotations in context, and that context is what lifts the near-chance signal into our strongest reward source. 

5.4 RL Training Results 

The RL rows of Table 4 report the Qwen3-4B, Qwen3-8B, and Nemotron-3-Nano-4B models, each trained with the naive agent prompt and one of three reward sources, the CI-RL string-matching reward Lan et al. (2025), the trained gen-RM (Section 4.3), and the annotation-conditioned reward (Section 4.2). The annotation-conditioned reward is the strongest of the three, bringing a 4B open-weight model within reach of proprietary frontier models on contextual privacy. 

Balancing privacy and helpfulness. Every reward source raises the clean rate of every base model under both prompts, and the annotation-conditioned reward attains the best clean rate in every family, even under the privacy-enhanced prompt the models were not directly trained on. With this reward, the open-weight Nemotron-3-Nano-4B reaches a 32.6% clean rate under the naive prompt, within a few points of proprietary frontier models such as Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite (35.4%) and Gemini 3.1 Pro (37.3%). The annotation-conditioned reward raises clean rates by reducing leakage while generally avoiding withholding more task-relevant information. The trained gen-RM still improves every base model’s clean rate, and on Nemotron-3-Nano-4B it cuts leaks the furthest of any reward, to 27.8% under the naive prompt, the lowest leak rate of any open-weight model. The trade-off is omissions. The reward model likely learns to focus on leaks far more than on omits, allowing trained models to suppress leakage by withholding more, so its omit rates climb to 59 to 70% and its clean rates stay below the annotation-conditioned reward’s. 

Comparison to CI-RL. We also compare against CI-RL end-to-end, training each base model with its rule-based string-matching reward (Lan et al., 2025) on its own GPT-4-generated synthetic dataset. For a fair comparison we use the same training hyperparameters sweep as our own models and select the best checkpoint by the same procedure, highest clean rate on the Gemma-4-31B-it dev holdout (Table 12), so the gap reflects the reward and its data rather than tuning. The string-matching reward does work, lowering leak rates and 10 raising every base model’s clean rate, but it is consistently weaker than the annotation-conditioned reward, which wins in every family on both prompts (27.3 vs. 24.1 for Qwen3-4B, 28.1 vs. 25.7 for Qwen3-8B, and 32.6 vs. 21.9 for Nemotron-3-Nano-4B under the naive prompt). Like the gen-RM, it lowers leaks largely by withholding task-relevant information. We document the structural limits of this substring-matching reward in Appendix G. We do not empirically compare against PrivAct (Cheng et al., 2026), another recent contextual privacy training method. It is trained with DPO rather than RL, and releases neither model checkpoints nor the preference data it trains on, so we can neither run nor reproduce it for a like-for-like comparison. We instead analyze its supervision in Appendix H. 

Generalization to held-out benchmarks. The annotation-conditioned models also generalize to PrivacyLens and CIMemories, two external benchmarks they never trained on, improving over their base models on both. We report and discuss these results, with caveats about the benchmarks’ model-generated labels, in Appendix F. 

# 6 Conclusion 

Agentic privacy is a contextual decision problem, and fully removing humans from the loop obscures the norms that define appropriate disclosure. Whether a given disclosure is acceptable, and which omissions a user would have wanted included, depend on human norms and expectations that single LLM judges cannot reliably assess yet. Aligning agents to privacy preferences requires keeping humans in the loop both at evaluation and training time. We introduced PrivacyAlign , a benchmark and training corpus of privacy-sensitive agent scenarios paired with pairwise human preferences, per-response leak and omit annotations, and free-text rationales from 599 unique annotators. Conditioning an LLM judge on same-prompt annotations from this corpus sharply reduces inter-judge disagreement and brings the judges into closer alignment with carefully audited gold labels. In this work, we also proposed annotation-conditioned rewards. Training small open-weight agents with RL using annotation-conditioned rewards substantially raises clean rates on PrivacyAlign , producing responses that protect sensitive information without dropping what the user needs, and the gains transfer. Our models also improve over their base models on both leakage and helpfulness on the held-out PrivacyLens and CIMemories benchmarks. Taken together, our results show that human annotations for privacy-sensitive scenarios can provide reusable, context-specific guidance for evaluating and training agents to make privacy decisions that better reflect human norms. 

# Limitations 

Synthetic scenarios. PrivacyAlign scenarios are generated by LLMs, not drawn from real user traces, because collecting and sharing authentic privacy-sensitive agent interactions would itself be a privacy violation. Our generation pipeline targets diverse domains, toolkits, and action types and validates that frontier models actually leak on its outputs. Nonetheless, synthetic data might struggle with realism and data quality as a result of the weaknesses of the models used for generation. 

LLM-judge dependence. Evaluation still relies on LLM judges, even with annotation conditioning. The judges may inherit bias and limitations from their underlying models, and while we have shown that conditioning on annotations partially corrects differences, judge agreement is still imperfect. 

Human-annotator heterogeneity. Prolific annotators differ in privacy intuitions, values, effort, and annota-tion ability. Inter-annotator κ is moderate on leaks (0.56) and lower on omits (0.12). This heterogeneity may be a property of the problem rather than noise to average away, but it means neither our gold labels nor the reward signal can exactly match any individual user’s preferences. Privacy norms may be pluralistic, not just noisy, so aggregating preferences into a single reward can smooth over real and legitimate disagreement. 11 Model scale. Training experiments use small open-weight checkpoints ranging from 4B to 8B parameters (Qwen3-4B, Qwen3-8B, and Nemotron-3-Nano-4B) because of limited computational resources. Experimenting with training larger scale agents remains an important next step. 

Deployment caution. Strong scores on PrivacyAlign do not guarantee perfect privacy handling in agents. Trained agents may still fail under distribution shift, adversarial prompts, or scenarios outside our coverage. 

# Ethical Considerations 

Annotator welfare. Annotations were collected through Prolific (annotator pool and eligibility criteria in Appendix K). Annotators were compensated at £10 per hour, consented to the use of their judgments and rationales, and provided no personally identifying information. 

Synthetic data. All PrivacyAlign scenarios are generated, not collected. No real users, real tool-call traces, or real personal data appear in the dataset. This is a deliberate choice that lets us release the corpus openly without leaking real users’ private information. 

Whose privacy norms? Although our 599 unique annotators span more than 20 countries of residence, the pool is restricted to English-fluent participants and is not representative of the global distribution of users. Privacy norms may differ across cultures and contexts, and an agent trained against this distribution may underweight the expectations of users from underrepresented backgrounds. 

Dual use. A public benchmark that exposes how agents leak could in principle be repurposed adversarially. We judge the defensive value of shared evaluation to outweigh this risk. The underlying leakage tendency is already prevalent in these models, and measurable progress requires a shared benchmark, data, and analysis.

# D Healthcare Domain Collapse 

When generating privacy-sensitive scenarios from scratch, all three generator models we tested (gpt-oss-120b, Qwen3.5-397B-A17B, Nemotron-3-Super-120B-A12B) gravitated toward health- and medical-themed contexts. Left unchecked, the corpus would concentrate disproportionately on healthcare. We call this failure mode healthcare domain collapse. The same pattern appears in Privasis (Kim et al., 2026), whose records carry coarse domain tags ( medical ,

legal , admin , comms , hr , finance ). We ask how often medical content leaks into records that lack the 

medical tag. Treating any row without medical in its record_tags as non-medical, we scan its text against a category vocabulary covering mental health, care or treatment, providers or patients, medical conditions, and reproductive health. To suppress keyword false positives, terms with common non-medical senses must appear in compound form ( patient record , treatment plan , psychotherapy rather than bare patient , treatment ,

therapy , or counsel ), and we drop rows whose only signal is the routine blood_type field or whose context is animal or veterinary. Of the 1,293,611 records, 81.3% are non-medical-tagged, yet 28.1% of those (295,690 rows) still carry substantive medical content. Matches concentrate in providers or patients (143,479), mental health (100,220), care or treatment (99,082), and medical conditions (84,866), and fall mostly under the legal (108,956), admin (77,763), and comms (58,107) tags. One row tagged only admin , with record_type “Recorded minutes from Faculty of Science postgraduate academic board meeting”, carries an attribute literally named “Mental Health Diagnosis and Treatment Details”. The medical tag clearly does not capture every record with medical content. 22 We guard our own pipeline against this in two ways. The diversity sampler caps any single domain at 25% of the corpus and any specific domain signature at 5%. A postprocessing filter then rejects any scenario whose declared domains omit healthcare but whose story, user instruction, tool trajectory, or final action invokes a healthcare toolkit (EpicFHIR, Teladoc) or any of roughly 50 healthcare-related strings (for example, HIPAA, mental health, prenatal). 

# E Limits of Automated Privacy Judgments 

Privacy judgments are inherently human-centered. What counts as appropriate disclosure depends on the recipient, the relationship, the social context, and the specific person doing the disclosing. Current LLMs are not yet able to make these judgments reliably on their own. Benchmarks that delegate to them use the labeling model’s opinions in place of human judgment. We illustrate this on two popular benchmarks. 

CIMemories. CIMemories (Mireshghallah et al., 2025) does not release its per-attribute necessary-vs-private labels publicly, so we replicate them by running GPT-5.5 with low reasoning to generate each label in the benchmark’s released script. The resulting labels frequently make calls that a thoughtful human, weighing real-world consequences, would dispute. In the context (Potential Employer, “Discuss employment history gaps”), the labels mark “The cited violation was improper medication documentation” as necessary for the candidate to disclose, even though volunteering a prior medical-documentation violation to a prospective employer is troubling. In the context (Performance Review Committee, “Explain productivity changes”), the labels mark “I have had zero days of absenteeism since becoming sober” as necessary, even though disclosing addiction recovery at a performance review could be a risk. In the context (Admissions Committee, “Address academic performance variation”), the labels mark therapy attendance and anxiety scores as necessary for the applicant to share with the committee. These labels are debatable, and they can overlook real-world consequences that a human would weigh. Any benchmark that uses such labels as ground truth grades agents against the labeling model’s own judgments rather than human ones. 

PrivacyLens. PrivacyLens (Shao et al., 2024a) uses Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, an early-2024 open model, to extract per-trajectory sensitive_info_items and to judge whether the agent’s final action leaks any of them. Beyond the dated extractor, we find a more structural issue. The labeled sensitive items routinely include exactly the content the recipient has explicitly requested. In sample main160 , the user emails a fertility clinic “with the details they need for our consultation”, and the consultation dates she has available, the questions she has prepared for the doctor, and her partner’s relevant family medical history are all flagged as sensitive. In cases like this, scoring well generally requires ignoring the user’s literal instruction, so the metric can reward refusal of legitimate requests. 

Our approach is not fully exempt. Our own method does not fully escape these limits. Human annotators are imperfect. They disagree, especially about omissions (Section 5), and any individual label can be wrong. Our reward and evaluation also still run through LLM judges, which carry biases of their own. What changes is where the privacy judgment comes from. Instead of substituting the labeling model’s unaided opinion for human input, we condition the judge on multiple same-prompt human annotations, anchoring its decisions to what real people considered sensitive or necessary in that scenario. This does not eliminate the noise, but it measurably tightens inter-judge agreement and agreement with the authors’ carefully annotated gold labels (Section 5) and keeps a human signal in the loop rather than replacing it with the model’s own unaided judgment.

CIMemories releases no ground-truth labels, so the notion of a violation is itself model-defined: GPT-5.5 role-plays each persona to decide which stored items must stay private and which may be shared, and is then scored against its own labels. This circularity should favor the label-generating model, and consistent with it GPT-5.5 attains the lowest violation rate (2.6%). The completeness metric, however, runs the other way: even the Qwen3-8B base model includes more of the task-relevant items (47.4%) than GPT-5.5 (46.3%), and its trained model more still (49.0%). The pattern is difficult to reconcile with the labels as a faithful target. We 24 therefore read the external-benchmark numbers as supporting evidence that our models generalize, rather than as precise measurements. 

# G Critique of CI-RL Training Supervision 

CI-RL (Lan et al., 2025) trains LLMs with reinforcement learning using a reward derived from per-instance lists of allowed (rewarded) and disallowed (penalized) strings. 1 To calculate reward, it is checked whether the literal allowed and disallowed strings appear in the agent’s output. The dataset itself, including the allowed and disallowed string lists that define the reward, is synthetic and was generated by GPT-4, so the supervision targets are LLM-produced rather than grounded in human judgments. This supervision has two further structural problems that limit what the trained model can learn. 

Same-sample contradictions. The reward presupposes a clean partition between strings that should be emitted and strings that should be suppressed. In 28 of 729 rows (3.8%), an identical normalized value appears in both the allowed and disallowed lists of the same row. In another 9 rows (1.2%), one side’s value is a substring of the other side’s value in the same row. On these 37 rows (5.1%) the substring-matching reward gives conflicting signals. The same characters in the response are simultaneously rewarded and penalized. For example, in a hotel-booking scenario the dataset lists Participants: “250 attendees” as allowed and 

Preparation notes: “250 attendees” as disallowed. In a spa inquiry it lists specific_spa_interest: “Deep Tissue Massage” as allowed and past_communication_2: “Deep Tissue Massage” as disallowed. 

Substring matching is fragile and bypassed by paraphrase. Even where labels are internally consistent, substring membership is a fragile proxy for the disclosure it is meant to capture. 10.3% of disallowed entries normalize to four characters or fewer ( “john” , “202” , “976” , “1234” ), which may fire on unrelated text. In the other direction, any paraphrase of a disallowed value (summary, indirect reference, paraphrased numeral, restructured sentence) avoids the match without changing the disclosure a human reader would identify, and any paraphrase of an allowed value is penalized as a missing rewarded item. The reward measures lexical overlap with a generated string list, not contextual appropriateness of the disclosure. 

# H Critique of PrivAct Training Supervision 

PrivAct (Cheng et al., 2026) trains models with Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) on pairwise preferences generated by a multi-agent LLM pipeline over PrivacyLens scenarios. The supervision has two issues. 

The DPO target inherits PrivacyLens failures. The per-scenario sensitive_info_items sets that drive PrivAct’s privacy signal are PrivacyLens’s own, extracted by Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, an early-2024 open model. As we document in Appendix E, those sets frequently mark the same content the user’s instruction explicitly asks the agent to share. PrivAct further substitutes LLM judges for human annotators when turning these labels into (chosen, rejected) pairs, so the DPO target is the labeling pipeline’s verdict over its own extracted items. 

Only non-reasoning models are considered. PrivAct uses DPO on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, and Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507, all non-reasoning models. The training method was not tested on reasoning-model agents.

# K Annotator Pool 

Annotations were collected through Prolific. We restricted the annotator pool to participants who (i) had completed at least an undergraduate degree, (ii) were fluent in English, (iii) had a Prolific approval rate of at least 99%, (iv) had completed at least 200 prior submissions on Prolific, and (v) were at least 21 years of age. Each annotator was permitted to complete only a single submission of 6 samples, which ensures annotation diversity across the dataset. Annotators were compensated at £10 per hour. The final PrivacyAlign dataset draws on 599 unique annotators whose countries of residence span more than 20 countries. 

# L Annotation Interface 

We collected annotations through a custom web interface served to Prolific participants. Figure 7 shows the full annotation page that an annotator sees for each item, comprising the scenario header (user instruction, story, data subject, data recipient, domains), the two candidate assistant responses side-by-side, the per-response leak/omit checkboxes, the preference selector, the AI-generated comparative analysis, and the free-text 26 explanation box. Figures 8 and 9 show close-ups of the item-level labels with the preference selector, and of the free-text explanation box, respectively. 

AI-generated comparative analysis. To lower the cognitive load of comparing two long assistant responses, we show an AI-generated comparative analysis on the same page after annotators submit their initial annotations for that sample. The analysis is produced by an LLM that summarizes the information items present in each response and explicitly notes which items appear in one response but not the other. The model is not asked to make any privacy or relevance judgment. It only enumerates information content. To prevent it from anchoring annotators’ initial judgments, the analysis is hidden until after the annotator has submitted their initial leak/omit labels, preference selection, and free-text explanation. Once the analysis is shown, annotators may edit any of these before saving, so the analysis can prompt corrections of genuine oversights without anchoring the independent judgment they committed first. 

Submission quality controls. Before an annotation can be saved, the interface enforces two minimum-detail requirements. The free-text explanation must be at least 12 words, and each “Leaks Sensitive Information” or “Misses Relevant Information” box an annotator checks must be accompanied by a description of at least 6 words stating what is leaked or missing. The interface also raises dismissible warnings on likely-inconsistent submissions, flagging contradictions in the labels, such as preferring the response the annotator marked as the only one leaking sensitive information, selecting Tie when the two responses carry different leak or omit labels, or saving an item after fewer than 90 seconds of review. The minimum-detail rules are hard requirements, whereas the consistency warnings can be overridden but prompt the annotator to revisit their labels first.
