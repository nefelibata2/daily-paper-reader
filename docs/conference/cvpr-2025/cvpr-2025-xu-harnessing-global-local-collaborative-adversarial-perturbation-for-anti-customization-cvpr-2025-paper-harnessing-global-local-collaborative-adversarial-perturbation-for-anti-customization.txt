## Abstract 

Though achieving significant success in personalized im-age synthesis, Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) pose sub-stantial social risks caused by unauthorized misuse (e.g., face theft). To counter these threats, the Anti-Customization (AC) method that exploits adversarial perturbations was proposed. Unfortunately, existing AC methods show in-sufficient defense ability due to the ignorance of hierarchi-cal characteristics, i.e., global feature correlations and lo-cal facial attributes, leading to weak resistance to concept transfer and semantic theft in customization methods. To address these limitations, we are motivated to propose a 

Global-L ocal C ollaborate d Anti-Customization (GoodAC) framework to generate powerful adversarial perturbations by disturbing both feature correlations and facial attributes. To enhance the ability to resist concept transfer, we disrupt the spatial correlation of perceptual features that form the basis of model generation at a global level, thereby cre-ating highly concept-transfer-resistant adversarial camou-flage. To improve the ability to resist semantic theft, lever-aging the fact that facial attributes are personalized, we designed a personalized and precise facial attribute distor-tion strategy locally, focusing the attack on the individualвҖҷs image structure to generate strong camouflage. Extensive experiments on various customization methods, including Dreambooth and LoRA, have strongly demonstrated that our GoodAC outperforms other state-of-the-art approaches by large margins, e.g., over 50% improvements on ISM. 1

вҖў We propose a global-local collaborated AC framework that simultaneously disrupts global perceptual feature correlations and local precise facial attributes, enhancing resistance to both the concept transfer and semantic theft. 

вҖў Extensive experiments on multiple Customization meth-ods, including DreamBooth and LoRA, demonstrate the effectiveness of our method. Our approach reduces face similarity metrics (ISM) to below 10% , significantly out-performing state-of-the-art methods and providing better privacy protection across different training strategies. 

## 2. Related Work 

2.1. Customization of Latent Diffusion Models 

Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) [26, 34] have become a cornerstone of generative modeling [25]. Built upon a progressive denoising architecture, LDMs integrate Vari-ational Auto-Encoders (VAEs) [16] to shift the diffusion process from image space to latent space, effectively mit-igating overfitting at the pixel level [30] and significantly improving generation efficiency. Moreover, by incorporat-ing cross-attention mechanisms and the CLIP encoder [24], LDMs demonstrate superior cross-modal generation capa-bilities, surpassing Conditional GANs (CGANs) [20] in synthesizing high-fidelity images from textual descriptions with greater accuracy and flexibility. Customization refers to leveraging the powerful genera-tive capabilities of LDMs to mimic given reference images representing specific concepts and generate new images of these concepts in different scenarios. In order to customize LDMs for specific concepts, methods like DreamBooth [27] and LoRA [13] are widely used. DreamBooth [27] fine-tunes the entire model using a few images and a unique identifier to associate new concepts with text embeddings. It modifies the original loss Lcond to Ldb as follows:

Anti-customization Studies 

To prevent misuse of private images in customized LDMs, recent studies have explored adversarial techniques for pri-vacy protection [17, 18, 33, 35, 41]. By introducing subtle perturbations to images, these methods aim to hinder the modelвҖҷs ability to learn and transfer representations of tar-get images. Early works like Glaze [29] applied style trans-formations to protect knowledge in latent diffusion mod-els, while AdvDM [18] utilized the modelвҖҷs inherent rep-resentations for attacks. Anti-Dreambooth [33] incorpo-rated Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) [19] into latent dif-fusion model attacks, using substitute models to calculate adversarial perturbations and significantly improving attack success rates. More recent methods like SimAC [35] have leveraged differences in perturbation effectiveness at differ-ent timesteps, employing greedy algorithms to select opti-mal perturbations and further enhance attack success. However, these methods primarily focus on improving optimization efficiency, but overlook two critical factors in customization: new scenarios and fine-grained facial at-tributes [18, 33, 35]. This oversight limits the ability to cap-ture the contribution of hierarchical features to customiza-tion quality, resulting in suboptimal protection performance as well as limited transferability [36].

3.2. Framework Overview 

To generate adversarial camouflages with strong resistance to concept transfer and semantic theft, we propose a Global-local c ollaborate d Anti-Customization (GoodAC) frame-work. This framework aims to generate robust adversarial perturbations by collaboratively disrupting global percep-tual correlations and distorting the local face attributes. The overall framework is illustrated in Figure 2. To enhance resistance to concept transfer, inspired by existing studies, perceptual features serve as the founda-tion for image generation [32], and LDM heavily relies on spatial consistency in perceptual features to maintain iden-tity during concept transfer. Motivated by this insight, we propose to strengthen the robustness of protected images against concept transfer by globally disrupting spatial cor-relations within the perceptual feature space. Specifically, we extract feature maps from both the first down-sampling block and the last up-sampling block of the UNet, and em-ploy the block angle transformation to disrupt spatial con-nectivity within these feature maps. Based on this, we de-sign a loss function to optimize adversarial perturbations, effectively preventing the concept in protected images from being successfully transferred into novel scenarios. In terms of resisting semantic theft, considering that fine-grained semantic features such as facial characteristics are crucial for protection, it is essential to accurately identify these details. This allows us to apply stronger perturba-tions locally to critical image regions, thereby weakening the ability of LDM to extract detailed user-specific informa-tion from the images. Specifically, we employ text inversion module to obtain precise semantic descriptions as semantic guidance, combined with edge detection techniques to ac-curately extract facial details. Consequently, perturbations are effectively focused on these critical regions, limiting the modelвҖҷs capability to capture personalized and precise facial attributes, thereby increasing the difficulty of accurately re-producing details from the original images. 

3.3. Global Feature Correlation Disruption 

Perceptual features refer to critical feature representations extracted by deep learning models from specific objects during image processing. These features typically emerge within intermediate layers of the model, such as convo-lutional layers and residual blocks. Existing research has shown that perceptual features serve as the fundamental ba-sis for both recognition and generation tasks [32]. During customization, the Latent Diffusion Model leverages spatial correlations within these perceptual features to effectively transfer personalized subject concepts into new scenarios for image generation. Inspired by this mechanism, we pro-pose disrupting the spatial correlations within perceptual features to strengthen the resistance of anti-customization examples against such concept transfer. 13416 edit

3.4. Local Facial Attribute Distortion 

To enhance the resistance of anti-customization examples against semantic theft, existing AC research [35] has pri-marily focused on gradient-based optimization approaches, investigating the role of high-frequency image features in accelerating the optimization process. However, these methods have not effectively segmented or identified se-mantically meaningful information within images for tar-geted optimization, thereby exhibiting limited capability in mitigating semantic theft [2, 4, 8, 23]. Therefore, we aim to precisely locate and distort local facial attributes, thereby disrupting the LDMвҖҷs ability to learn fine-grained semantic information such as facial features, and ultimately enhanc-ing the effectiveness of anti-customization. Specifically, we propose Local Facial Attribute Dis-tortion that leverages adaptive semantic guidance and edge detection to target critical features within the anti-customization perturbations, consisting of two key steps: (1) generating more detailed descriptions of the protected images to guide the LDM in encoding richer internal fea-ture of fine-grained semantic attributes; and (2) applying 13417 edge detection between feature maps of adversarially cam-ouflaged and clean images during the perturbation process, and optimizing the perturbations by minimizing the L2 loss between the detected edges. We first employ a text inversion module 2 to generate fine-grained image descriptions, which serve as semantic guidance to enrich the internal features of the LDM. ## 4. Experiments 

4.1. Experimental Settings 

4.1.1. Datasets 

Following the settings established in previous studies [33, 35], we conduct experiments using two facial image datasets: CelebA-HQ [15] and VGGFace2 [3]. These datasets consist of images from approximately 50 individu-als, with 15 high-quality facial images available per individ-ual for customization purposes. The images of each individ-ual are divided into three groups: A, B, and C, each contain-ing five portrait images. Group A is used as clean reference facial images for model training, while group B represents the target images for generating anti-customization exam-ples. Group C is not utilized in the experiments. 

4.1.2. Models 

Given that the open-source Stable Diffusion is the most pop-ular LDM implementation in the community, we follow the experimental setup used in prior studies [33, 35] and con-duct model training and generate anti-customization exam-ples on SD-v2.1 [1], and quantitatively evaluate the effec-tiveness of anti-customization on SD-v2.1. Furthermore, to assess the performance of our method in a black-box set-ting, we qualitatively examine the effectiveness of the anti-customization examples generated on SD-v2.1 when ap-plied to SD-v1.4. The corresponding results are provided in the supplementary material.

4.1.4. Evaluation Metrics 

We adopt four evaluation metrics to assess the effectiveness of anti-customization. Specifically, we consider (1) Face Detection Failure Rate (FDFR), which quantifies whether a face can be detected in the generated image; (2) Identity Score Matching (ISM), which measures identity similarity between generated and original images; (3) SER-FQA, a facial image quality assessment metric; and (4) BRISQUE, a widely used general-purpose no-reference image quality metric. The detailed computation procedures for these met-rics are provided in the supplementary material. 

4.1.5. Detailed Experimental Settings 

We incorporate AC examples generation into DreamBooth training, with 50 steps and 6 PGD iterations per step. The perturbation budget О· is constrained to 16/255, and the step size Оұ is set to 5eвҲ’3. During testing, the DreamBooth model is fine-tuned on the generated AC examples with a batch size of 2, a learning rate of 5eвҲ’7, and 1000 training steps. Unless otherwise specified, the training and prior prompts are вҖңa photo of sks personвҖқ and вҖңa photo of personвҖқ . All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA L20 GPU.

## 6. Conclusion 

In summary, we propose GoodAC, which achieves sig-nificant progress in enhancing the effectiveness of anti-customization (AC) methods by collaboratively disrupting global perceptual correlations and distorting local facial at-tributes. Our GoodAC demonstrates strong defense capa-bility by resisting both concept transfer and semantic theft, outperforming existing state-of-the-art anti-customization approaches. Experimental results across various Latent Dif-fusion Models and customization methods show that our method provides superior protection against unauthorized facial usage, thereby advancing the standards of privacy and security in personalized image synthesis. Despite these promising advancements, our approach still has some limitations. For instance, GoodAC has only been evaluated on open-source, fine-tuning-based models, and its performance on proprietary commercial models or non-fine-tuned models remains unexplored. Moreover, the GoodAC framework, like other existing anti-customization methods, might be vulnerable to specially designed cus-tomization techniques, pointing to a critical future research direction вҖ” the development of robust anti-customization (AC) strategies, which deserves further investigation. 13421 References
