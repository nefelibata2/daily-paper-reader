## Abstract 

The proliferation of multi-modal generative models has in-troduced new privacy and security challenges, especially due to the risks of memorization and unintentional disclo-sure of sensitive information. This paper focuses on the vul-nerability of multi-modal image captioning models to mem-bership inference attacks (MIAs). These models, which syn-thesize textual descriptions from visual content, could inad-vertently reveal personal or proprietary data embedded in their training datasets. We explore the feasibility of MIAs in the context of such models. Specifically, our approach leverages a variance-based strategy tailored for image cap-tioning models, utilizing only image data without know-ing the corresponding caption. We introduce the means-of-variance threshold attack (MVTA) and confidence-based weakly supervised attack (C-WSA) based on the metric, means-of-variance (MV), to assess variability among vec-tor embeddings. Our experiments demonstrate that these models are susceptible to MIAs, indicating substantial pri-vacy risks. The effectiveness of our methods is validated through rigorous evaluations on these real-world models, confirming the practical implications of our findings. 

## 1. Introduction 

The advancement from traditional machine learning (ML) to advanced generative models has progressed significantly in recent years [7]. An example of this development is seen in the field of multi-modal models, which are able to pro-cess information from various modalities [4]. These models have become increasingly sophisticated and are employed in challenging tasks involving various types of data ( e.g ., text, images, video). In this paper we focus on image cap-tioning models [2, 3, 23, 24, 26, 34, 50], a multi-modal model used to generate textual descriptions for images. While multi-modal models have fostered the develop-ment of a variety of new multi-modal applications, they also present new privacy and security challenges [6, 28]. A crit-ical concern is the potential of generative models, particu-larly those trained on large private datasets, to inadvertently memorize and expose sensitive information. Such risks can lead to issues related to intellectual property misuse and pri-vacy breaches involving data from proprietary images or documents [5, 45]. Among the various threats are mem-bership inference attacks (MIAs), which aim to determine whether specific data records were included in a model’s training dataset [20]. The ability to infer membership can result in the exposure of personal health information, fi-nancial details, or undisclosed affiliations, thereby posing a threat to individual privacy and security. Initially, studies on MIAs focused on classification mod-els, revealing heightened privacy risks primarily due to model overfitting [9, 20, 47]. Subsequently, attention turned to generative models, where research highlighted their ten-dency to memorize training data, raising concerns about digital forgery and the loss of originality [10, 11, 39]. In MIAs, as defined by Shokri et al . [37], shadow mod-els are used to mimic the target model’s behavior. How-ever, crafting effective shadow models requires extensive datasets that mirror the original training data, along with substantial computational resources. Additionally, in many MIAs [14, 19, 21, 30, 33, 33, 43, 44, 49], the shadow models are scaled to hundreds of samples, and must share similar architectures with the target model and use identical train-ing algorithms. Their dependence on shadow model train-ing limits their applicability for large-scale models [21, 43]. To the best of our knowledge, the study performed by Hu et al . [21] is the only research exploring MIAs on im-age captioning models. The authors introduced two attack methods, which shadow models are employed. Further-more, they did not directly evaluate the methods on oper-ational image captioning models, such as those from Hug-ging Face. Instead, they were assessed in a simulated envi-ronment, using artificial models trained on synthetic config-urations, small datasets, and numerous training epochs. Recent studies have proposed MIAs that do not rely on shadow models [22, 25]. Specifically, Ko et al . [22] introduced the cosine similarity (CS) attack (CSA) and This CVPR paper is the Open Access version, provided by the Computer Vision Foundation. Except for this watermark, it is identical to the accepted version; the final published version of the proceedings is available on IEEE Xplore. 9210 the weakly supervised attack (WSA). These attacks target large-scale multi-modal contrastive language-image pre-training (CLIP) models [35] that utilize text-image data pairs. Both methods use a CS metric to measure the similar-ity between the embedding vectors of images and text from the target CLIP model. The second method, WSA, com-bines ground-truth data about non-members with a pseudo-member set, which consists of samples flagged as likely members based on their CS values. A classifier is trained on the dataset to distinguish members from non-members. In this paper, we introduce two MIAs targeting image captioning models: the means-of-variance (MV) thresh-old attack (MVTA) and the confidence-based weakly su-pervised attack (C-WSA). In contrast to the techniques of Ko et al . [22], we tailor our methods specifically for im-age captioning models and enhance them with a new met-ric, the MV metric, which measures the variance between vectors. Our methods use only images as input, operat-ing under the realistic assumption that an adversary lacks access to ground-truth reference text ( i.e ., captions) and in-stead uses captions generated by the target image captioning model. Additionally, we integrate a confidence threshold training method that leverages the pseudo-dataset’s confi-dence to improve the classifier’s performance. We evaluate the proposed methods under varying levels of attacker knowledge (zero and partial) in a black-box sce-nario. Additionally, we conduct the evaluation using pub-lic models trained on large-scale datasets to simulate a re-alistic setting. The evaluation results show that our pro-posed methods identify membership information in multi-modal image captioning models, using BLIP [23], ViT-GPT2 [13], and GIT [42], trained on large-scale datasets. Our methods enhance detection capabilities, achieving ap-proximately 20% to 30% improvements over current meth-ods. To summarize, our main contributions are as follows: • New MIAs targeting image captioning models: We in-troduce the MVTA and C-WSA for the image caption-ing domain, two new attacks in which the input consists solely of images. • The means-of-variance metric: We introduce a quan-titative metric designed to assess the variance between vectors in a multi-dimensional space. While we used it for texts, this metric is general and can be used in various fields that utilize embedding vectors. • Innovative training of the pseudo-member set: We in-troduce a new step in the process of training the attack model, a confidence-based training step that utilizes a pseudo-member set. • Real-world application: By evaluating our methods on Hugging Face models, we demonstrate the practicality and effectiveness of our methods in real-world scenarios. 

## 2. Related Work 

Previous studies on MIAs exploited the model’s tendency to produce more confident predictions for data included in the training set compared to unseen data [9, 20, 37, 40, 47], which is also referred to as memorization, since they tend to memorize the training data. MIAs are more challenging in the context of generative models, where confidence scores or prediction labels are typically unavailable [10, 11]. In ad-dition, it was shown that the time it takes to train a genera-tive model and the size of the dataset used to train the model influence the level of memorization, where extended train-ing epochs and smaller datasets typically lead to increased memorization, enhancing the success of attacks [39]. Recent studies have demonstrated diffusion models’ vul-nerability to MIAs [12, 46]. In white-box settings, tech-niques involving statistical and neural network-based infer-ence have shown high effectiveness, especially in models trained with small datasets and a large number of train-ing epochs [14, 19, 30]. In black-box scenarios, methods leveraging semantic fidelity and novel classifier-based ap-proaches have achieved high accuracy, though they often depend on access to non-training data and known training sets, which can limit their broader applicability [33, 44, 49]. 

Attacks on multi-modal models. The CLIP model [35], which is one of the many types of multi-model models, uti-lizes contrastive learning to align text captions with images, leveraging unsupervised learning to interpret and generate image content based on natural language descriptions. Tra-ditionally, MIAs targeting models like CLIP have relied on computationally intensive shadow models [14, 19, 21, 30, 33, 33, 43, 44, 49]. However, recent research has proposed efficient, shadow-free approaches for MIAs against multi-modal models. The textual unimodal detector [25] identi-fies the use of personally identifiable information (PII) in CLIP models using only textual data, reducing both com-putational costs and privacy risks without the need for im-ages. Another method, the WSA [22], addresses practi-cal MIAs against large-scale multi-modal CLIP models by combining ground-truth information about non-members with noisy member data from a weak attack to develop an attack model. The WSA starts by querying the target model for image and text features of each non-member and eval-uates the CS between these features. The CS distribution, typically Gaussian, helps approximate the mean and vari-ance and is used to flag samples with a CS significantly above the mean as likely members. This identified set, known as the pseudo-member set, is then used to construct an attack dataset for training an attack model that deter-mines the membership status of any target sample based on its features. Notably, the attack model does not account for the possibility that labels marked as true during train-ing might be mislabeled, which could potentially improve performance if some of these were excluded from training. 9211 Table 1. Another type of multi-modal model, BLIP [23, 24], ad-vances vision-language tasks by adapting to both under-standing and generation challenges, including image-text retrieval, image captioning, and visual question answering. To the best of our knowledge, the work of Hu et al . [21] is the only one that explores MIAs on image captioning mod-els. Their paper introduces two attack methods: metric-based and feature-based. The metric-based approach uses similarity metrics to infer membership status by comparing the similarity of model outputs with ground-truth data. The feature-based approach utilizes a pre-trained multi-modal feature extractor to analyze similarities between the input and output features. These attacks were not applied di-rectly to operational image captioning models but rather in a controlled setting with synthetic configurations, in terms of small dataset size and a large number of training epochs, us-ing an encoder-decoder framework to simulate the process. In addition, the authors also employed shadow models, which increases the complexity and computational costs. Unlike previous works, we focuses on the realm of multi-modal image captioning models, an area that has received little research attention in the context of MIAs. In this re-search, we avoid the conventional reliance on computation-ally intensive shadow models, which necessitate datasets that closely mirror the training data of the target model. Instead, we base our attack methodologies on metrics de-signed to detect discrepancies in data variance. By apply-ing MIAs in scenarios that only involve image data without access to corresponding textual information, our proposed MIAs have a broader range of applications. ## 3. Threat Model 

The primary goal of the attacker is to determine whether a given sample is a member of the target image caption-ing model’s training set or not. The attacker can query the model, submitting images and receiving the corresponding generated text captions in response, but does not have any access to the model itself ( i.e ., query-only). The attacker does not have any knowledge of the target model’s archi-tectural, parameters, or the specific training algorithm used. We emphasize that the attacker’s dataset consists solely of images, allowing the attack to remain effective even in scenarios where access to the model’s training captions or other textual references is limited or unavailable. We ex-plore two different settings concerning the attacker’s knowl-edge of the data used to train the target model: • Zero-knowledge setting: The attacker has no prior knowl-edge of the distribution of the data used to train the target model, making this approach challenging but realistic in restricted data access scenarios. • Partial-knowledge setting: Following prior research [9, 21, 22, 33, 43], the attacker has access to a publicly avail-able dataset of images, Dall , approximating the data dis-tribution used to train the target model. The attacker also has access to a non-member dataset Dno , while the membership status of Dall is unknown. The non-member dataset can be collected from content published after the model’s release or synthesized by image-generating AI models, such as publicly available diffusion models. 

## 4. Methodology 

Our methodology is designed to leverage our intuition re-garding the use of variance in the outputs of a target image captioning model as a means of inferring infer membership information. The pipeline of our methodology consists of three main stages: text generation and multi-modal feature encoding, variance metric computation, and application of proposed attacks. First, the target model generates text cap-tions from images. These captions and images are then en-coded into feature embeddings, using pre-trained text and image encoders, respectively. The outputs are then analyzed to determine membership, aiming to ascertain whether a new image was part of the target model’s training dataset. Next, we compute a variance-based metric, the MV met-ric, to quantify the variance among text embeddings gen-erated for each image. This metric serves as the basis for identifying membership by capturing the relationship be-tween variance and membership. Finally, we propose two attacks based on the MV metric: the MVTA and C-WSA. The MVTA operates in a zero-knowledge setting by exploit-9212 ing the reduced variance of text embeddings for training set images. The C-WSA, designed for a partial-knowledge set-ting, combines non-member ground truth with probabilis-tic pseudo-member labels and employs a confidence thresh-old to train an attack classifier model. The stages of our methodology are described below. 

4.1. Text Generation and Feature Encoding 

This stage involves two steps: text generation and multi-modal feature encoding, as shown in Figure 1. Typi-cally, image captioning models use two text generation de-coding strategies to generate concise descriptions: beam search [32] and top-p sampling [18]. Beam search is de-terministic, consistently generating the same outputs for a given input by keeping the best candidates when choosing the next word. In contrast, top-p sampling, also known as nucleus sampling, is non-deterministic and introduces di-versity by selecting from a probabilistic ”nucleus” of op-tions. Carlini et al . [8] showed that when trained on paired data of the prompt and image, an image generation model generates similar images from the same prompt for two dif-ferent random seeds. Following this principle, we denote the caption generated for an image x using top-p sampling by the model Gen as Gen (x). Then, if multiple captions generated for the same image x are highly similar under a distance measure d (Gen (x) ≈d Gen (x)), it suggests that the model has memorized the image. Based on this, we as-sume that the variance among the n captions generated for an image will be lower if the target model has been trained on that image. During the feature encoding step, features are derived us-ing a pretrained text encoder ftext to obtain text embeddings from the generated captions, and a pre-trained image en-coder fimg to extract visual features from images. We note that the widespread availability of large-scale pre-trained models allows adversaries to easily obtain both encoders.

4.2. Means-of-Variance Metric 

Based on our intuition, we introduce the new MV metric, which is designed to quantify the variance between embed-ding vectors. This metric constructs a variance vector of size m, where each index represents the variance of a spe-cific feature calculated across n vector embeddings. Once the variance vector is constructed, the metric then computes the mean of the values within it. The resulting single value represents the overall variance among the vectors, providing a concise measure of dispersion. Intu-itively, if a sample’s M V is substantially lower than μno ,specifically by more than λσ no for a given constant λ, it suggests that the sample is likely a member. For the dataset Dall , which comprises potential mem-bers, we compute the MV scores using the text embeddings generated by the target model. Samples in Dall whose MV scores significantly deviate from this non-member baseline are identified as potential members. Specifically, if a sam-ple’s MV score exceeds a defined threshold calculated as 

μno − λσ no , it is classified as a pseudo-member. The cor-responding subset of these identified samples ( Dp−mem ), serves as our pseudo-member set. This set comprises a mix of likely members and potential false positives. The formulas associated with this process are as follows: 

τ = μno − λσ no (4) 

Dp−mem = {x | M V (ftext (Gen (x))) < τ, x ∈ Dall } (5) With the Dp−mem and Dno in place, we proceed to create the attack training set Dattack , comprising image features from Dno ∪ Dp−mem with their associated binary labels: 

Dattack = {fimg (x)|x∈Dno ∪Dp−mem ,b =1[x∈Dp−mem ]} (6) where fimg (x) represents the image features extracted by the image encoder fimg for each image x, and b is a bi-nary indicator with 1 representing a potential member and 0 representing a non-member. Finally, using Dall , we train the attack classifier model fattack so that it is capable of determining the membership status of any target sample by evaluating the sample’s image features. In Dall , samples labeled with 0 are definitively non-members, whereas those labeled with 1 may not actually be members. This distinction guides our training strategy for the attack classifier model fattack , which incorporates a confidence threshold. Initially, the attack model undergoes a ”warm-up” phase, training without interruptions to estab-lish baseline prediction tendencies. This phase lasts for a predefined ratio of epochs, referred to as ”warm-epoch.” Then, before each training batch, we evaluate the mem-bership confidence of the samples. For those identified as pseudo-members ( b = 1 ), training continues only if their membership confidence exceeds a predetermined threshold. This approach refines the training process, focusing primar-ily on samples most likely to be members, thus enhancing the accuracy and effectiveness of our attack classifier. 

## 5. Evaluation 5.1. Evaluation Setup Datasets. To evaluate our attacks, we utilize a compos-ite of datasets including MSCOCO [27], FLICKR30k [48], Textcaps [38], IAPR TC-12 [17], and Nocaps [1] as Dall .These datasets contain approximately 113K, 30K 28K, 20K, and 15K images, respectively. Additionally, we gener-ate artificial dataset images using Stable Diffusion v1.5 [36] based on the captions of FLICKR30k to simulate the cre-ation of a new non-member dataset for the target model. In practice, the target models are trained on one dataset, and we sample from the combination of the other datasets to es-tablish ground-truth non-members. We then further divide these non-members into two disjoint subsets: one provides the ground-truth non-member data as Dno , and another is utilized to assess the attack’s performance. In the evaluation phase, both member and non-member data are split into training and testing sets. The training set is used as Dall , while the testing set is used to evaluate the attack model classifier. Our approach deviates from the traditional MIA evaluation setup, which typically involves splitting a single dataset into member and non-member sub-sets for small-scale classification models [21, 37]. Instead, similar to Ko et al . [22], we opted for a configuration that offers greater real-world relevance and practical impact. 9214 Target models. We evaluate our attacks using three promi-nent public image captioning models from Hugging Face: BLIP Large [23], ViT-GPT2 [13], and GIT Base [42], all trained on large-scale datasets. 

Evaluation metrics. We employ several key metrics to as-sess how effective our methods are: area under the curve (AUC), accuracy (ACC), and true positive rate (TPR) at a low false positive rate (FPR), which was used by [9].

## 6. Conclusions and Future Work 

We introduce two MIAs, MVTA and C-WSA, specifi-cally designed for image captioning models in the zero-knowledge and partial-knowledge settings, respectively. These attacks leverage the MV metric, which serves as the foundation of our approach. The results obtained in our ex-periments highlight the vulnerability of image captioning models to MIAs and emphasize the privacy implications in-herent in these models. Our evaluation showed that the ef-fectiveness of our attacks is influenced by the text genera-tion decoding strategies employed and the number of cap-tions generated. Specifically, the diversity introduced by top-p sampling and the variance in the number of gener-ated captions provide a more informative set of features for MIAs. These factors affect our attacks’ ability to distin-guish between member and non-member data. Future research could focus on: (1) developing and eval-uating techniques to mitigate the effectiveness of the MVTA and C-WSA; (2) expanding the attacks’ applicability to a wider range of models, such as diffusion models, by adapt-ing the MV metric for use on different types of data (this would only require changes to the encoders). 9217 References
