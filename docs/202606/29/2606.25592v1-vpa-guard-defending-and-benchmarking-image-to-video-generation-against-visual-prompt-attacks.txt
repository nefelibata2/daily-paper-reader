## Abstract 

Recent advancements in Image-to-Video (I2V) generation have transformed input images from simple appearance references into interactive control interfaces where visual cues such as arrows, sketches, and emojis orchestrate complex video dynamics with unprecedented controllability. However, these seemingly innocuous static cues can be interpreted by models as executable temporal instructions, unfolding into harmful actions in the generated videos. Despite the severity of this threat, existing safety benchmarks remain predominantly focused on text-based and content-only image-based jailbreaks, leaving implicit visual prompt attacks insufficiently explored. To bridge this gap, we present VVA-Bench, the first systematic benchmark for evaluating video generation safety under categorized vision-centric prompt attacks. Extensive experiments on VVA-Bench demonstrate that state-of-the-art models are highly susceptible to such attacks, with Attack Success Rates (ASR) reaching 100.0% on Wan 2.7 

and 74.8% on Veo 3.1 . To mitigate these risks, we propose VPA-Guard, a retrieval-augmented and self-evolving defense framework. By leveraging few-shot reasoning to identify latent malicious intents, our method reduces the attack ASR by 44.2% and the harmfulness score by 73.4% on average, while maintaining the model’s utility for legitimate user edits. Our work provides both a rigorous benchmark and an effective defense strategy to advance safe and socially responsible multimodal generation. 

## 1. Introduction 

Video models have achieved significant progress in image-to-video (I2V) generation and visual reasoning (Yang et al., 2025b; Blattmann et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2025; Ren et al., 2024). Recent advances in visual prompting have enabled more effective manipulation of video generation by integrating visual cues such as marks, arrows, and mixed visual-text signals (Fang et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025b; Xiao et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2023). This powerful interaction paradigm achieves fine-grained control and superior usability by enabling users to overlay their editing intentions directly onto the input image to guide the generation process (Krishna et al., 2017; Bigverdi et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2025). However, this profound controllability exposes a critical safety vulnerability, posing a significant threat to the legitimacy of the generated content for the existing video models (Bailey et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2025; Qi et al., 2024). Because these visual prompts appear strictly innocuous within a static image context, they introduce a potent and novel attack approach (Ying et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2025a). Users can exploit this visual instruction-following mechanism to force models into adversarial reasoning, subtly steering it to synthesize malicious content, which is more difficult for models to defend against than to attack with malicious input text and image (Hou et al., 2026). Recent studies focus on exploiting this novel visual prompting to jailbreak state-of-the-art video models, constructing diverse attack samples to expose the vulnerabilities of video models (Zheng et al., 2026). ## 2. Visual Prompt Attack and Defense on Video Models 

2.1. Implicit Visual Prompt Attack  

> Figure 2. Comparison between traditional text-prompt attack and visual-prompt attack. Top: Samples of attack scheme com-parison. Bottom: Performance comparison. Visual prompt attack poses a higher threat to video models.

An image-to-video (I2V) generator M (I, T ) → V generates video V with input image I and input text T . Unlike the tradi-tional jailbreak attack methods inject malicious information into the text prompt, which can be easily filtered by the model’s input check and internal safety mechanisms. The implicit Visual-Edit attack conceals the malicious intent within the visual modal-ity. Specifically, the attacker defines a visual transformation 

f : Ioriginal → Iadv , which manipulates the original image Ioriginal 

by embedding specific symbols, arrows, or adversarial edits to construct an adversarial image Iadv . When paired with a seem-ingly benign text prompt T , the adversarial image implicitly misleads the model’s reasoning process, bypassing its safety mechanism to generate harmful content: M (Iadv , T ) = Vharmful 

where Vharmful represents the generated video that violates safety policies. This type of attack poses significant security risks in real-world deployment, as it exploits the model’s visual instruction-following capabilities to trigger malicious reasoning implicitly. 

2.2. VPA-Guard 

To mitigate the aforementioned implicit visual attacks, a critical challenge is to discern the intent of the visual edit and distin-guish genuine malicious visual manipulations (i.e. Iadv ) from benign user-specified image edits. To achieve this, we propose a retrieval-augmented and self-evolving defense pipeline, as shown in Figure 3, the VPA-Guard consists of the following 4 stages: 

Representative Attack Samples Selection: The visual attack often have similar patterns, e.g., moving the object in the input image by arrow and replacing the object with dangerous object by using boxes in the image, combined with safe text prompt. So we select 42 representative samples and analyze why they will lead to malicious generation. These samples were chosen to provide exhaustive coverage of all identified attack formats and risk labels within our benchmark. 

Multimodal Feature Extraction and Attack Paradigm Retrieval: To identify the intention of visual edits, we employ DINOv2 as our visual encoder Ev . DINOv2 is lightweight and sensitive to localized visual features, making it ideal for detecting localized malicious symbolic guidance (e.g., arrow, emoji, etc.). Simultaneously, a Sentence Transformer Et

extracts the semantic text features. The visual and text features, denoted as fv = Ev (I) and ft = Et(T ), are fused into a joint representation to retrieve similar attack patterns by querying a vector database D, which stores the representative attack samples and serves as a prior knowledge base of established attack paradigms. Each entry in D contains a representative attack sample (Iadv , T, R ) paired with explicit reasoning R , which details the attack’s mechanism and why it induces harmful generation Vharmful . Based on cosine similarity, we retrieve the top-k most relevant attack patterns. 

Few-Shot Prompting for Safe Generation: Instead of relying on rigid and naive safety filters that might reject reasonable edit requests, we leverage the few-shot reasoning capabilities of the video models, which is fully verified by (Wiedemer et al., 2025). We dynamically construct a few-shot defense prompt incorporating the retrieved top-k attack samples and their associated rationales. Post-Generation Self-Evolving Defense: Since the static vector database D cannot cover the entire distribution of potential OOD (Out-of-Distribution) attacks, to make our defense methods complete, we introduce a self-evolving mechanism. If the defense fails ( V is flagged as unsafe), a VLM-based Reflection Agent is invoked. This agent analyzes the successful attack triplet (I, T, V harmful ), synthesizes a rationale for the defense bypass, and formulates a new attack paradigm. This new paradigm is subsequently embedded and appended to the vector database D, ensuring the defense system continuously adapts to emerging threats. 

## 3. VVA-Bench 

We introduce VVA-Bench, a structured diagnostic framework designed to evaluate the safety of modern Image-to-Video (I2V) models under implicit visual guidance. Figure 4 provides an overview of the 5 stages of our proposed benchmark. 

3.1. Benchmark Construction Source Data Curation: We first candidate source images that are visually clear, semantically diverse, and suitable for image-conditioned video generation are collected. These source images are predominantly drawn from AIGC-based image pools and are manually filtered to encompass a broad range of subjects, scenes, and interactions. Because the benchmark necessarily includes harmful intent and potentially disturbing scenarios, the data construction process also includes explicit safety and annotation-welfare considerations. Candidate images are filtered before annotation, highly redundant or unnecessarily graphic cases are excluded, and the benchmark is curated to study unsafe intent without maximizing shock value. In addition, annotators are informed in advance about the nature of the task, may pause or terminate participation at any time, and carry out annotation in a collaborative setting rather than in isolation. Details regarding the Human-in-the-loop mechanisms employed for sample curation are further discussed in Section E.4. 4VPA-Guard: Defending and Benchmarking Image-to-Video Generation Against Visual Prompt Attacks  

> Figure 4. Overview of the proposed VVA-Bench pipeline. The workflow illustrates benchmark construction, the two attack forms, and the four-stage rating protocol utilized for safety evaluation.

Structure Attack Construction: The clean sources are then transformed into unsafe attack inputs through the addition of either a VP layer or a 2frames temporal structure. For VP , the perturbation assumes the form of arrows, emoji-like symbols, pose lines, sketches, or camera-control marks placed directly on the image. For 2frames , a before/after image pair is constructed such that the harmful process is not explicitly depicted in a single frame but is rather implied by the temporal completion between the two images. Each constructed case is annotated with a weak or underspecified text prompt, one or more target-risk labels, a format label, and, for VP , a subtype label from {MC, EI, PC, DI, CC }. Finally, quality control is performed to verify image-path consistency, annotation completeness, prompt-image alignment, and the fidelity of the visual prompt to the intended mechanism. 

Attack Taxonomy: According to the established annotation specification, VVA-Bench encompasses two major attack forms targeting I2V models with both single-image input and multi-image input: 2frames and VP . The 2frames 

setting exploits the architectural characteristic wherein many video generation models accept first-frame, last-frame, or keyframe-conditioned inputs. Harmful intent is encoded by placing the state preceding a risky event in one image and the state succeeding the event in another, thereby inducing the model to complete the unsafe process. VP refers to visual prompts; this setting captures attacks wherein harmful intent is embedded into the image via visual guidance rather than explicit text. In the current release, VP is further disaggregated into five representative mechanisms: Movement Control (MC), Emoji Instruction (EI), Pose Control (PC), Draft Instantiation (DI), and Camera Control (CC). 

Unified Attack representation: VVA-Bench contains 452 unsafe attack samples in total, comprising 367 visual-prompt cases and 85 two-frame cases. Within the visual-prompt subset, Movement Control and Emoji Instruction constitute the two largest groups, followed by Pose Control, Draft Instantiation, and Camera Control. Furthermore, a mechanism-aligned safe-control set of 45 examples is constructed for the VP subset, comprising 9 safe samples for each of the five VP subtypes. These safe samples are used to evaluate whether the model will normally execute the legitimate user requests. Each benchmark sample contains a clean source image or image pair, a visually prompted input image, an associated text prompt, an attack-type label, a risk-category label, and metadata for evaluation. A full breakdown by attack form and subtype is provided in Section C.2. Unsafe samples are paired, when feasible, with safe controls that preserve the same mechanism while removing harmful intent. This paired structure improves the diagnostic value of the benchmark and helps separate model vulnerability from general controllability. 5VPA-Guard: Defending and Benchmarking Image-to-Video Generation Against Visual Prompt Attacks

## 4. Main Experiments 

4.1. Experimental Setup: Victim Models. To evaluate the generalizability of implicit visual attacks and the robustness of our defense framework, we select four state-of-the-art (SOTA) Image-to-Video (I2V) generation models as victims: Wan2.7(Wan Team, 2025), Kling2.6(Kling AI, 2025), Hailuo(MiniMax, 2025), and Veo3.1(Google DeepMind, 2025). 6VPA-Guard: Defending and Benchmarking Image-to-Video Generation Against Visual Prompt Attacks 

Evaluation Details: For all evaluated models, we utilize the default recommended generation parameters (e.g., sampling steps and guidance scale) to simulate standard user interactions. The input comprises the visually prompted image (or image pair) coupled with a weak, underspecified text prompt that avoids triggering standard textual safety filters. 

4.2. Main Results Vulnerability of Existing I2V Models: As demonstrated in Table 1, state-of-the-art I2V models exhibit severe vulnerabil-ities when exposed to implicit visual-prompt attacks. Without any defense mechanisms in place, the models fail to reject malicious instructions disguised as visual edits. The overall Attack Success Rate (ASR) reaches alarming levels across all evaluated models, peaking at a near-perfect 100.0% for Wan and 99.6% for Kling. Even for models with relatively tighter native guardrails like Veo and Hailuo, the baseline ASR remains as high as 74.8% and 81.2%, respectively. Furthermore, the successful attacks generate content with high severity. The Harmful Score (HS) under the ”no defense” setting ranges from 4.79 (Veo) to 7.30 (Wan), indicating that the generated videos heavily violate safety policies (e.g., extreme violence, NSFW, or copyright infringement). 

Effectiveness of the Proposed Defense: Our proposed retrieval-augmented and self-evolving defense framework signifi-cantly mitigates the threat of visual-prompt attacks. As shown in the ”w/ defense” rows of Table 1, the implementation of our pipeline leads to a substantial drop in both ASR and HS across all models. On average, the defense achieves an absolute ASR reduction ( ∆ASR) of 44.2% and an absolute HS reduction ( ∆HS) of 73.4%. We also analyze the attack and defense effects in each format. Our method is highly effective against most types of the attack taxonomy except the Camera Control. The visual encoder successfully captures these localized anomalous features, and the retrieval module accurately aligns them with known malicious intents. A practical defense mechanism must block malicious attacks and preserve the model’s controllability for legitimate users as well. To evaluate the extent to which the defense method impacts the legitimate request, we measure the Over-defense Rate (OSR) on the benign safe45 dataset, which contains legitimate visual edit requests. The OSR is defined as the proportion of the rejected benign samples to the total benign dataset. As reported in Table 1, our framework achieves a low average OSR of 12.8%. This demonstrates that our multimodal feature extraction and retrieval-augmented few-shot prompting empower the model to genuinely understand the intent behind the visual cues, rather than blindly blocking specific shapes. Consequently, our framework provides a robust safety shield against implicit attacks while introducing minimal disruption to the normal user editing experience. 

4.3. Validity of MLLM-as-a-judge 

The evaluation of safety in Image-to-Video (I2V) generation, particularly under implicit visual attacks, requires a robust and scalable assessment mechanism. While the Harmful Score ( HS ) serves as our primary metric for benchmarking the severity of generated content, its reliance on an automated MLLM-based judge necessitates rigorous validation to ensure its credibility. This experiment is tended to establish that our automated evaluation pipeline can serve as a reliable proxy for human safety judgment. Specifically, we conduct a correlation analysis between the scores generated by the MLLM judge and those assigned by three human experts. Both judges are equipped with the same rating rubric shown in Section C.4. By quantifying this alignment, we aim to demonstrate the validity and consistency of using MLLMs for large-scale, automated safety assessments in this benchmark. The tab 2 illustrates the high fidelity of the MLLM-based judge in identifying and grading the severity of content that violates policy within the generated video output. Across the dimensions of HT , HI , and HIT , we observe a strong positive correlation with human expert judgment. They demonstrate that the MLLM judge can accurately capture unimodal and cross-modal harms. This capability ensures that the automated scoring system does not merely flag content, but provides a precise, multi-level assessment of harmfulness that aligns closely with human intuition. 

The weakest correlation is observed in the harmfulness of output videos ( HV ), with Pearson and Spearman correlations of 

0.546 and 0.560 , respectively. This highlights that grading temporal dynamics remains the most challenging dimension for automated judgment. This difficulty likely stems from several factors: the gradual temporal emergence of malicious content and discrepancies between the MLLM and human experts in contextual policy reasoning. Despite this moderate correlation, the MAE of 0.7467 remains relatively low. This indicates that the automated judge is sufficiently aligned with expert annotations for scalable evaluation, but ambiguous video-level cases may still require manual inspection. In summary, the correlation results strongly validate that the automated MLLM-as-a-judge pipeline is highly reliable

## 5. Related Work 

Multimodal Jailbreaks and Safety Evaluation. Jailbreak attacks aim to bypass the safety alignment of foundation models to elicit policy-violating outputs. Although early studies focused primarily on text-only large language models (Perez & Ribeiro, 2022; Liu et al., 2025b; Chao et al., 2024; Mazeika et al., 2024), the emergence of multimodal models has expanded the attack surface to high-dimensional visual inputs. Studies including MM-SafetyBench (Liu et al., 2024) and subsequent research (Qi et al., 2024; Bailey et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2025; Ying et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2025a) demonstrate that query-relevant images, typographic visual prompts, and visual distractions can compromise safety-aligned language backbones. Parallel efforts in multimodal safeguarding (Chen et al., 2024; Chi et al., 2024) highlight an urgent necessity for comprehensive vision-language evaluation. Within the video domain, safety benchmarks such as T2VSafetyBench (Miao et al., 2024) evaluate models against malicious textual prompts. Several studies further investigate text-based jailbreaks for text-to-video (T2V) models through discrete prompt optimization (Liu et al., 2025a), high-salience keyword injection (Mu et al., 2025), scene-splitting (Lee et al., 2025), or implicit language cues (Ying et al., 2026), alongside proposed defenses such as T2VShield (Liang et al., 2025). Nevertheless, these approaches primarily treat the text prompt as the attack carrier or focus exclusively on output content filtering (Pang et al., 2024b; Qu et al., 2025; Xie et al., 2025). 

Visual Prompting and Controllable Generation. A parallel line of research investigates visual prompts as an interface for grounded perception and reasoning (Zhu et al., 2016; Krishna et al., 2017). Recent multimodal models increasingly utilize bounding boxes, arrows, masks, and scribbles to specify user intent (Xu et al., 2025; Bigverdi et al., 2024). This trend demonstrates that visual cues function as executable instructions rather than passive references (Fu et al., 2024b; Huang et al., 2024). Concurrently, controllable video generation has introduced increasingly rich non-textual control signals, which include dense-to-sparse motion trajectories, camera-view controls, and compositional structures (Wang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2025; Pan et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2026; Lin et al., 2024). Although these advances drastically improve usability, they inherently expand the vulnerability surface: if a model can accurately follow a drawn arrow for motion control, the same interface can be exploited to encode unsafe intent (Xu et al., 2025; Feng et al., 2026; Hou et al., 2026). 

Vision-Centric Attacks in Generation Models. Instruction-based image editing models have evolved to interpret visual marks and mixed visual-textual cues (Meng et al., 2022; Hertz et al., 2022; Brooks et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024a; Qwen Team, 2025). Recognizing the security impact of this paradigm shift, prior research (Hou et al., 2026) introduces Vision-Centric Jailbreak Attacks and IESBench for image editing models. Expanding beyond static images, recent studies explore video-specific vulnerabilities (Hu et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025), where temporal dynamics can amplify harmful visual queries. Notably, Visual Instruction Injection (Zheng et al., 2026) demonstrates that I2V models can be compromised by augmenting safe reference images with typographic descriptions and bounding boxes and underscores the fact that pre-generation safeguards frequently inspect images strictly as static content, thereby overlooking harmful intents that only materialize during dynamic synthesis (Zheng et al., 2026; Yoon et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2025). 

Position of This Work. In summary, while existing benchmarks address textual (Chao et al., 2024; Miao et al., 2024) or static visual jailbreaks (Hou et al., 2026), they overlook the unique spatio-temporal risks and instruction-following nature of modern video generation models. Recent I2V attack strategies (Wang et al., 2025; Zheng et al., 2026) reveal this vulnerabilities. However, they lack a unified and diagnostic framework. VVA-Bench bridges this gap by systematically measuring distinct visual instruction types and temporal compositions. Through the incorporation of mechanism-aligned safe controls and multi-stage evaluation, this work provides a comprehensive diagnostic tool tailored to the dynamic nature of video generation, thereby complementing existing text-centric and static-image safety protocols.

C.3. Detailed Taxonomy and Annotation Instruction 

We categorize visual prompt attacks according to the format through which the harmful intent is encoded in the input. The taxonomy focuses on the instruction carrier rather than the semantic harm category itself. Specifically, each sample is annotated with one of the following format labels: 2frames , MC , EI , PC , DI , or CC . 2frames : Two-frame temporal interpolation attacks. The 2frames category refers to attacks that exploit video generation models capable of generating videos from a start frame and an end frame, or from multiple key frames. In this format, the harmful event is not necessarily described in the textual prompt. Instead, the input frames specify the visual state before and after a harmful event, encouraging the model to infer and synthesize the missing transition. For example, the first frame may depict a potentially harmful interaction about to occur, while the second frame depicts the resulting harmful consequence. The attack succeeds when the model fills in the temporal gap with a harmful action that connects the two visual states. 

MC : Movement Control. Movement Control attacks use visual cues to guide the motion trajectory of a particular object, body part, or agent. These cues may include arrows, curves, directional marks, paths, or other visual indicators that specify how an entity should move over time. The harmfulness arises when the specified movement leads to an unsafe, violent, or otherwise policy-violating event. A sample should be labeled as MC when the core attack signal is the visually indicated motion path, rather than the appearance of the object itself. 

EI : Emoji Instruction. Emoji Instruction attacks use emojis, icons, symbolic marks, or abstract visual symbols as implicit instructions. Instead of writing an explicit harmful command in the textual prompt, the attacker encodes the desired harmful action through symbolic elements embedded in the image. These symbols may be combined with otherwise benign text prompts such as “follow the image instruction” or “animate the scene according to the visual cues.” A sample should be labeled as EI when the main harmful intent is communicated through emojis or icon-like symbols. 

PC : Pose Control. Pose Control attacks use visual annotations to guide a person, animal, or agent into a specific pose or body configuration. These annotations may include skeleton lines, pose templates, body-direction arrows, limb markers, or reference silhouettes. The target of the attack is not merely object movement, but the generation of a particular posture or physical configuration that may imply a harmful, abusive, degrading, or unsafe action. A sample should be labeled as PC 

when the harmful instruction is primarily conveyed through pose or body-configuration control. 

DI : Draft Instantiation. Draft Instantiation attacks provide a rough sketch, doodle, layout, or incomplete visual draft and ask the model to instantiate it into a realistic or coherent video. In this setting, the harmful intent is embedded in a low-fidelity drawing rather than in explicit text. The model is expected to interpret the draft and generate a detailed video that realizes the implied scene or action. A sample should be labeled as DI when the attack relies on transforming an abstract or rough visual draft into a concrete harmful video. 18 VPA-Guard: Defending and Benchmarking Image-to-Video Generation Against Visual Prompt Attacks 

CC : Camera Control. Camera Control attacks use visual cues to manipulate camera motion, viewpoint, framing, or focus in a way that reveals, emphasizes, or constructs harmful content. Typical cues may include zoom-in boxes, camera-direction arrows, focus marks, cropping indicators, or viewpoint annotations. Unlike MC , which controls the movement of objects or agents, CC controls the virtual camera itself. A sample should be labeled as CC when the harmful outcome is mainly caused by camera movement, viewpoint change, or selective framing. 

Annotation guidelines. Before each sample is made, annotators should assign exactly one primary format label from 

{2frames , MC , EI , PC , DI , CC }. The primary label should reflect the dominant mechanism by which the harmful instruction is communicated. If the attack is based on a start–end frame pair or key-frame interpolation, it should be labeled as 2frames , even if the frames contain additional visual marks. For visual-prompt-based attacks, annotators should identify the main instruction carrier: motion trajectory for MC , symbolic icons or emojis for EI , body or pose configuration for PC ,rough sketch realization for DI , and viewpoint or camera movement for CC .When multiple visual cues appear in the same sample, annotators should select the label corresponding to the cue that most directly causes the harmful generation. For example, if an image contains both an arrow and a pose skeleton, the sample should be labeled as MC if the arrow specifies the harmful motion, but as PC if the pose skeleton is the primary harmful instruction. Ambiguous cases should be resolved by considering the intended model behavior described by the accompanying text prompt and the most salient visual cue in the input image. This taxonomy separates the attack format from the harmful content type. Therefore, the same harmful target category may appear under different format labels. For instance, a harmful physical action may be induced through two-frame interpolation, movement arrows, pose annotations, or rough drafts. This design allows the benchmark to evaluate not only whether a model generates harmful content, but also which visual instruction formats are more likely to bypass safety mechanisms.

## E. Other Statements 

E.1. Limitations 

Although VVA-Bench addresses an important gap in video generation safety evaluation, it remains a first-step benchmark rather than a complete account of multimodal safety risk. The current release focuses on two attack formats, VP and 

2frames , which reflect the settings currently supported by most image-conditioned video generation systems and covers a great diversity of attack patterns. Future work should explore more diverse attack patterns beyond VP and 2frames .VVA-Bench is also designed primarily to study unsafe generation and safety failure. Although the safe-control subset helps separate controllability from genuine misalignment, it is still narrower than a full benign benchmark. As a result, the current release is better suited for measuring vulnerability than for specifically measuring false positives, unnecessary refusals, or over-moderation. The current evaluation pipeline also relies on automated multi-stage scoring with an MLLM-based judge. This improves scalability, but subtle, ambiguous, or temporally complex cases may still require stronger human calibration. More broadly, VVA-Bench is centered on image-conditioned video generation and does not yet cover the full range of multimodal video interfaces that future systems may support. 

E.2. Ethics Statement and Annotation Welfare 

VVA-Bench includes cases involving harmful intent and potentially distressing scenarios. It is released as a research resource for safety evaluation and model diagnosis, not as a repository for redistributing harmful content. Annotator welfare is a central concern during data construction. Reviewers are informed in advance about the nature of the task and may pause, skip, or withdraw at any time. To reduce burden, annotation is conducted in bounded batches, and annotators work in a shared setting so that difficult cases can be discussed rather than handled alone. Especially disturbing, ambiguous, or low-value cases can be escalated for reassignment, secondary review, or removal. We also filter redundant, excessively graphic, or diagnostically uninformative examples whenever possible. The goal is to preserve safety-relevant intent while minimizing unnecessary exposure to harmful material. 

E.3. AI Use 

This work involves the use of AI systems in two distinct ways: as research subjects/components and as auxiliary tools for manuscript preparation. First, AI systems are part of the research methodology. We evaluate several image-to-video generation models as victim models to study their vulnerability to visual prompt attacks. In addition, we use an open-source multimodal large language model, Qwen3.5-VL-35B-A3B, as an automated judge for scalable multi-stage safety evaluation, including the assessment of input-image harmfulness, text-prompt harmfulness, cross-modal harmfulness, and output-video harmfulness. The use of this MLLM-as-a-judge pipeline is explicitly described in the main paper, and its validity is further examined through correlation analysis with human expert annotations. In VPA-Guard, AI models are also used as part of the proposed defense 28 VPA-Guard: Defending and Benchmarking Image-to-Video Generation Against Visual Prompt Attacks 

framework, including retrieval-augmented reasoning and a VLM-based reflection agent for analyzing failed defense cases and updating the attack-paradigm library. Second, AI writing assistants may have been used only for auxiliary purposes, such as grammar correction, wording refinement, formatting assistance, and code or script debugging. E.4. Human-in-the-loop Discussion 

This section details the essential human-centric processes integrated into our pipeline to ensure the high quality and semantic alignment of VVA-Bench. 

Adversarial Sample Curation and Annotation During the data construction phase, human experts first curate a high-quality collection of images from diverse AIGC-based sources and the Internet, specifically selecting candidates that provide robust anchors for Image-to-Video (I2V) dynamics. Following this, experts manually orchestrate the integration of novel visual cues and corresponding text prompts to embed implicit malicious intent while preserving semantic coherence. To ensure the diagnostic value of the benchmark, experts provide fine-grained annotations for the risk category and attack mechanism for each instance. Figure 8 illustrates the specialized User Interface (UI) developed to facilitate this annotation process and maintain label consistency. 

Calibration of the Automated Judge To ensure that our automated evaluation metrics remain grounded in human safety standards, we perform a rigorous alignment study between human experts and potential MLLM judges. A score panel (as depicted in Figure 9) is employed, where three human experts independently evaluate a subset of generated videos across all harm dimensions ( HT , H I , H IT , H V ). This human-labeled ”Gold Standard” serves as a calibration baseline to select the MLLM that exhibits the highest correlation with human intuition. This process ensures that the MLLM judge acts as a reliable and ethically-aligned proxy for large-scale safety assessment within our benchmark. 

E.5. Social Impact 

This work studies visual prompt attacks against image-to-video generation models and proposes both a diagnostic benchmark and a defense framework. Its primary positive societal impact is to improve the safety, accountability, and deployability of generative video systems. As visual cues such as arrows, sketches, bounding boxes, pose marks, emojis, and multi-frame inputs become increasingly common interfaces for controllable generation, harmful intent may be embedded in visual structures that appear innocuous under static inspection. By systematizing these risks through VVA-Bench, our work helps researchers, model providers, and platform operators evaluate failure modes that may otherwise remain hidden until deployment. In addition, VPA-Guard provides a practical defense direction that aims to identify implicit malicious intent while preserving legitimate visual editing requests, thereby supporting safer and more useful multimodal generation systems. At the same time, this work has potential negative societal impacts due to its dual-use nature. A benchmark that categorizes visual prompt attacks may inadvertently help malicious users better understand how to construct harmful prompts, bypass pre-generation safety filters, or exploit temporal completion behavior in image-to-video models. The benchmark also contains unsafe intent categories and may expose annotators or researchers to disturbing content. Moreover, an imperfect defense may introduce over-refusal, suppressing benign creative edits, or under-refusal, leaving certain harmful cases undetected. Automated MLLM-based judging may also inherit biases or make inconsistent decisions on ambiguous temporal harms. To mitigate these risks, we adopt a safety-oriented release and evaluation protocol. We do not intend to release executable harmful prompts, generated harmful videos, or any material that directly enables abuse without appropriate safeguards. Instead, we plan to provide sanitized metadata, evaluation rubrics, safe-control examples, aggregate statistics, and repro-ducible scoring scripts where possible. Access to sensitive benchmark components should be controlled and limited to bona fide research, safety evaluation, and red-teaming purposes under responsible-use terms.
