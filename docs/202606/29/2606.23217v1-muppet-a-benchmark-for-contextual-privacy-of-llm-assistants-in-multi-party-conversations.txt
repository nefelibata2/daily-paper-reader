Abstract 

LLM agents are increasingly deployed in multi-party environments, handling sensitive personal data on behalf of individual users, for instance in group chats. When such an agent discloses private information, it reaches every group member at once. This risk is structurally harder to control than in one-to-one settings, as every piece of private information must be appropri-ate for every recipient in the group. Yet all existing contextual privacy benchmarks con-sider only single-interlocutor settings, leaving multi-party privacy risks unmeasured. We intro-duce M UPPET ( Mu lti-Party Privacy Exposure 

Testing), a benchmark for contextual privacy in multi-party conversations. Our experiments show that models leak substantially more in multi-party settings than one-to-one evalua-tions suggest. Frontier models are vulnerable, and smaller open-weights models, often pre-ferred for local deployment with sensitive data, even more so. Existing contextual privacy de-fences offer only partial protection, degrade utility, and do not resolve the underlying party-tracking problem. 

1 Introduction 

Large language model (LLM) agents are now de-ployed in shared, multi-user environments. An agent embedded in a team Slack channel may have access to a user’s calendar, direct messages, and health records, and speak on that user’s behalf to a dozen colleagues. Every response the agent gen-erates is visible to all group members. A single piece of private information, surfaced in the wrong context, leaks to twelve people at once. Contextual privacy benchmarks, grounded in the theory of contextual integrity (Nissenbaum, 2004), evaluate whether LLMs respect social norms gov-erning information flow. 2 Background and Related Work 

Privacy research on LLMs has evolved from study-ing training-data leakage to examining how models handle sensitive information at inference time, and more recently to the challenges posed by agentic and multi-agent systems. MuPPET extends this line of work to multi-party environments that prior benchmarks have not addressed.  

> 1The source code and data in this work are publicly avail-able at: https://github.com/elenasofia98/MuPPET .

From memorisation to contextual privacy. 

Early work on LLM privacy focused on leakage from training data due to memorisation (Carlini et al., 2021, 2023; Mireshghallah et al., 2022; Nasr et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023). More recent studies investigate contextual privacy , i.e., leaks during inference when models process sensitive inputs (Staab et al., 2024; Ghalebikesabi et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024; Green et al., 2025; Goel et al., 2026). 

Contextual Integrity (CI) (Nissenbaum, 2004) pro-vides the foundation of contextual privacy: privacy is respected when information flows according to the norms governing a specific social context. 

Benchmarks for privacy protection following CI theory Several benchmarks operationalise CI for LLMs. ConfAIDE (Mireshghallah et al., 2024) in-troduced a four-tier setup showing a gap between privacy knowledge and behaviour. Similar find-ings appear in PrivacyLens (Shao et al., 2024), Privacy in Action (Wang et al., 2025), and CI-Bench (Cheng et al., 2024). Those benchmark tests LLM in an agentic setting, with access to memo-ries, tools, and external interactions. With a spe-cific focus on memories, CIMemories (Mireshghal-lah et al., 2026) studies leaks that might occur be-cause of persistent memories, while AgentDAM (Zharmagambetov et al., 2026) and Bagdasarian et al. (2024) show that tool-using agents can in-advertently or adversarially leak sensitive data. AgentSCOPE (Ngong et al., 2026) further high-lights that violations often occur in intermediate tool-use traces even when final outputs appear safe. However, these studies primarily consider single-agent, one-on-one interactions. 

Multi-agent settings. Recent benchmarks ex-plore interactions among multiple agents. MAG-PIE (Juneja et al., 2025) evaluates collaborative tasks, ConVerse (Gomaa et al., 2026) studies ad-versarial multi-turn agent communication, and AgentLeak (Yagoubi et al., 2026) examines inter-mediate reasoning and traces in multi-agent sys-tems. However, they still assume structured inter-actions and do not fully capture privacy risks in open multi-party environments where information circulates across overlapping social contexts. 

Multi-party assistants. AgentSocialBench (Wang and Jiang, 2026) is a parallel effort to ours, studying one-on-one and small-group settings (3–6 participants with explicit roles). In MuPPET, we evaluate agents’ ability to infer implicit social norms in complex multi-party scenarios with many participants. 

3 MuPPET: A Benchmark for Testing LLMs in Multi-Party Conversations 

We propose MuPPET (Mu lti-Party Privacy 

Exposure Testing) to measure privacy risks when LLMs act as personal assistants in multi-party con-versations. In MuPPET, an LLM acts on behalf of a specific user within a group of coworkers and is given access to background information about that user, including memories from prior one-on-one conversations with other team mem-bers. These memories contain sensitive informa-tion shared within a limited social context, as well as the user’s constraints and preferences. The model is then placed in a group conversation where it must contribute while respecting implicit bound-aries on information flow. MuPPET thus evaluates whether the model can distinguish between infor-mation that is appropriate to share with the broader group and information that should remain confined to its original context. 

3.1 Dataset Composition and Construction 

MuPPET is composed of 562 multi-party conver-sations in English, where a model is allowed to speak on behalf of one of the users. The conversa-tions involve multiple team members, coordinating activities in a workplace scenario. Each conver-sation ends with a question for the model, which has to answer based on its user preferences and goals. The model can access its user’s background information, which we will refer to as the model 

memories , that contains both private information revealed by the user in some other context and their goals, preferences, and constraints. Dataset items are synthetically generated from manually curated seeds, contextualised to produce multi-party conversations reflecting each seed’s sce-nario. User groups are created to instantiate the seeds, with different roles distributed across 11 types of work environments. Given a seed contain-ing a conversation topic, the private information of one user, and an organisational question, we fol-low a two-step process: first generating a blueprint ,then the actual conversation messages. A complete dataset sample is provided in Appendix A.2. 

Seeds. Seeds simulate workplace interactions in which sensitive personal information could be indi-rectly disclosed through business communications. Each seed is structured around three components: a high-level work scenario (e.g., scheduling, per-formance evaluation, or travel arrangements), a 

sensitive attribute associated with an individual (such as health conditions, family responsibilities, or immigration status), and a legitimate managerial or collaborative question . The question could in-advertently elicit sensitive information if answered carelessly. A complete list of seeds is provided in Appendix A.3.1. 

User groups. User groups instantiate the seeds with realistic synthetic workers drawn from the PANORAMA dataset (Selvam and Ghosh, 2025), which provides large-scale identity records with realistic demographic and occupational attributes. Individual occupational qualifications are first grouped into broader organisational departments (e.g., Engineering, Healthcare, Finance, Educa-tion), and the population is filtered to include only individuals of working age. Users are then grouped based on both department and geographic location 3to create populations that are consistent from a re-gional and professional standpoint. Within each department-location subset, individuals are ran-domly shuffled and divided into fixed-size teams of 20 members. The resulting dataset consists of 11 homogeneous working groups of 20 users. For each group, a user is selected at random: this user will be our target user, on whose behalf the LLM assistant will speak. 

Blueprints. The blueprint generation phase trans-forms each seed into a structured work scenario with rich per-user descriptions. Each seed is expanded using Gemini 2.5 Flash with a sys-tem prompt requiring third-person, non-dialogic narration from an external observer’s perspec-tive (full prompts in Appendix A.3.2). Given a user group and seed, the model generates team-level notes (conversation goal and coordination) and individual-level notes (constraints, priorities, and planning factors for each member). When individual-level notes for the target user include sensitive information, they resemble summaries of past interactions with colleagues, embedding the sensitive data through its practical work implica-tions such as availability, workload, or scheduling. These notes form the memories the assistant ac-cesses when replying in the group conversation, en-abling us to test whether models respect contextual privacy boundaries. Quality checks confirm struc-tural compliance, seed fidelity, and n-gram diver-sity; after filtering, 562 of the initial 583 generated items are retained. A full analysis is in Appendix A.4. 

Multi-party conversations. The multi-party con-versations step transforms each blueprint into a realistic multi-turn group chat by reconstructing a plausible interaction history consistent with the outcomes already defined in the blueprint. All con-versations are generated with Gemini 2.5 Flash (full instructions in Appendix A.3.3). Shared notes define the conversation trajectory, while individual-level notes shape each participant’s priorities and behaviour. The conversation also includes the LLM assistant, constrained to act strictly on behalf of the target employee. The sequence ends with a human participant’s question addressed to the assistant, matching the seed question. We verify coherence and seed consistency using an LLM-as-a-judge, supplemented by manual review of flagged cases; all 562 items are retained (Appendix A.4). 

3.2 Metrics 

We measure privacy leakage and utility in model responses. A privacy leak occurs when the re-sponse reveals private user information; since no explicit sharing preferences are given, any disclo-sure counts as a leak. We also evaluate utility, as the privacy-utility trade-off is central to contex-tual privacy research (Mireshghallah et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Mireshghallah et al., 2026; Zhar-magambetov et al., 2026): a model that maximally protects privacy may simply refuse to answer, be-coming useless. 

Privacy. Since private information might be ex-plicitly generated by the assistant or could be easily inferred from its response, in MuPPET we apply an LLM-as-a-judge for leak evaluation. The judge is grounded in contextual integrity: it receives a private information topic, source sen-tences from the memories that mix private and non-private content, and a model-generated re-sponse (full instructions for the judge in Appendix A.5.1). It identifies the specific private details in the sources and checks whether the response reveals the same information either directly or through a strong semantic implication. A response is labelled as a privacy violation only when it clearly com-municates the private information or conveys an equivalent meaning. The use of an LLM-as-a-judge to detect privacy leaks has been documented and validated by a large body of literature (Shao et al., 2024; Mireshghallah et al., 2026). In Appendix A.6.1 we further discuss how to ensure that this approach is sound in our setting. 

Utility. In MuPPET, the utility evaluation mea-sures whether the model uses memories to produce responses that align with the user’s preferences, ex-pectations, or practical constraints. The memories ,in fact, contain both sensitive personal informa-tion and contextual details that may help the model answer effectively. In MuPPET, a highly useful model should extract those preferences from the 

memories and use them appropriately in the re-sponse. For example, a memory about a chronic health condition may contain preferences to avoid extra workload or demanding schedules. The use-ful behaviour is therefore the ability to infer stable preferences or constraints from prior interactions while avoiding unnecessary disclosure of the un-derlying sensitive details. We also employ an LLM-as-a-judge in this set-4ting, as detailed in Appendix A.5.2. The judge first decomposes each of the memories and identifies useful information only. Then, for each conversa-tion, the model’s answers are analysed to determine whether they use or do not use the provided useful information: if at least one piece of useful infor-mation is reported in the model answer, the overall answer is labelled as useful. We will report the percentage of useful answers as our utility metric. The soundness of this approach is further discussed in Appendix A.6.2. 

4 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate both open-weight and closed frontier language models. The open-weight models include instruction-tuned models like Meta-Llama 3 and 3.1 8B (Grattafiori et al., 2024), as well as Qwen 3 thinking models at 4B, 8B, and 14B scales (Yang et al., 2025). We also include frontier proprietary models, namely GPT-5.5 (OpenAI, 2026) and Gem-ini 2.5 Pro (Comanici et al., 2025), to compare performance across capability levels and deploy-ment settings. The prompting setup is designed to simulate multi-party conversational environments in which multiple users interact over time while the LLM assistant retains access to memory sum-maries. Each interaction is structured through a system prompt and a user prompt, where the entire conversation up to that point is presented. The sys-tem prompt defines the agent identity, the identities of the users involved, the memories containing sum-maries from prior background conversations, and a shared summary of the group conversation so far (as detailed in Section 3.1). The user prompt con-tains the message history together with the current message that requires a response from the LLM as-sistant. A complete description of the test instruc-tions and additional details on the experimental setup are provided in Appendix A.7.

In the one-on-one setting, the model is instructed that only the user who sent the last message will see the response, as if the assistant were responding within a private conversation with that user. As the best-case scenario, and consistent with other benchmarks that specify user preferences, we also include a note stating that the user is uncomfortable sharing highly sensitive information. In multi-user conversations, the note specifies which participants should not receive that information. In one-on-one conversations, it states that the assistant should avoid discussing it with the current recipient. The two settings, from a communication point of view, are equivalent: the recipient (or the recipients) of the generated answer should not get access to the sensitive information. This finding suggests that current LLMs struggle to correctly define privacy boundaries when multiple users are involved in an interaction. While in one-on-one conversations the assistant needs to account for only a single recipient, conversations with multiple participants require keeping track of which partic-ipants are aware of a given piece of information and which should be excluded from it. This behaviour raises concerns about the deployment of conversational assistants in complex social environments, where multiple users and over-lapping privacy boundaries are common. Smaller open-source models appear particularly vulnera-ble , as they exhibit substantially higher information leakage rates compared to state-of-the-art propri-etary systems. This trade-off suggests that models that are best at extracting useful information also tend to use sensitive data to provide more context-rich answers. Frontier models, in general, achieve the best balance between privacy and utility. Gemini 2.5 Pro has the lowest leakage rate, with a utility score which is higher than that of several open-weight models with significantly lower privacy per-formance. GPT 5.5 has the highest utility score while maintaining a lower leakage rate than all open-weight models. Overall, the results show that privacy leakage in multi-party conversations is a major risk across all tested LLMs: in fact, all evaluated models leak sensitive information in a substantial fraction of the conversations. This finding highlights the impor-tance of more effective mechanisms for selective memory usage and privacy-aware contextual rea-soning.

This limitation is particularly concerning given the growing adoption of local small language models for handling sensi-tive personal data, where weaker privacy reasoning capabilities may increase the risk of information disclosure despite the perceived benefits of local deployment. 

5.4 Error Analysis 

We design a set of probe tasks to analyse possible causes of the leakage rates observed on MuPPET. Rather than asking models to complete the original task, we evaluate specific capabilities related to audience awareness and contextual privacy. We consider four probe tasks (on a subset of 200 dataset items), at increasing levels of difficulty, that require the model to analyse the multi-party con-7versation, along with memories and shared context. The user tracking probe tests whether the model can correctly identify participants in the conver-sation. Knowledge attribution tests whether the model can infer which users already know the sen-sitive information. User-level contextual privacy 

tests whether the model can decide if sensitive in-formation should be shared with a specific user. 

Group-level contextual privacy tests whether the model can determine if sharing sensitive informa-tion with the whole conversation group is appropri-ate We aim to attribute each leak to a possible cause, examining the simplest answers first: we hypoth-esise that if a leak occurs and, on the same input, the model also fails a simple probe task, then it is likely that any failures on the other, more difficult probes may be influenced by the failure on the first, simpler one. Therefore, we attribute the leak to the failure of the simplest probe task that fails, in increasing order of difficulty. This produces a non-overlapping decomposition of benchmark leaks for each of the considered possible causes.

The error attri-bution analysis reveals how smaller models lose track of the users in the conversation, while for more capa-ble models leaks increasingly stem from unresolved group-level privacy constraints or a gap between privacy understanding and action.

The plot in Figure 3 presents the decomposition of benchmark leaks into the sequence of increas-ingly complex probing error categories. The re-maining category, labelled “Benchmark only”, con-tains benchmark leaks that are not explained by any evaluated probes. For items that are not attributed to any of the probes, the model likely understands users’ roles and contextual privacy implications, yet leaks some private information. This is an in-stance of the gap between knowledge and action (Mireshghallah et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2025) observed in LLMs’ behaviour. The decomposition reveals substantial differ-ences across model families. The instruction-tuned Llama models attribute most leaks to simple user tracking failures. In contrast, the reasoning models exhibit a markedly different pattern. User tracking errors decrease sharply with scale, while most at-tributed leaks shift towards user-level contextual privacy. This indicates that stronger reasoning mod-els handle basic conversational structure more reli-ably, yet still fail when privacy-sensitive inferences require deeper contextual reasoning. Frontier mod-els show a further shift, attributing the majority of the leaks to failures in identifying group-level privacy constraints or leaving the majority of leaks unexplained. The increasing proportion of “Benchmark only” items highlights that, as models improve, fewer leaks can be attributed to failures in user tracking or contextual understanding. This suggests that bench-mark failures in stronger models are not primarily caused by missing knowledge or reasoning ability. Rather, they reflect a gap between knowledge and action, where the model appears to understand user roles and privacy implications in multi-party con-texts, but still produces privacy-violating outputs in complex conversational settings. 

6 Conclusions 

We introduced MuPPET, a benchmark designed to evaluate contextual privacy risks in multi-party conversational settings. Our results demonstrate that multi-party privacy leakage is a distinct and previously under-measured failure mode in current contextual privacy testing of LLM-based assistants: multi-party conversations entail complex social in-teraction that significantly increases the risk of sen-sitive information leak in inappropriate contexts. We further show that all evaluated models, by de-fault, exhibit a high degree of leakage. Notably, smaller open-weight models are consistently the most vulnerable. This is particularly concerning given their widespread use in privacy-sensitive de-ployments, where stronger assumptions of data pro-tection are often made. Existing contextual privacy defences only partially mitigate leakages, and nega-tively impact the model’s utility. Addressing multi-party leakage will likely require new modelling paradigms for handling multiple sources of infor-mation, with the specific objective to account for complex information flows within shared contexts. 8Limitations 

MuPPET focuses on professional team settings, where workplace norms and organisational roles structure information flow in relatively well-defined ways. Multi-party privacy in other con-texts, such as family groups, friend networks, or patient communities, may involve different and more implicit norm structures. The privacy chal-lenges specific to those settings are not covered by this benchmark, and extending the evaluation to such contexts is a natural direction for future work. The dataset is synthetically generated from man-ually curated seeds using a large language model. While quality checks confirm structural compliance and seed fidelity, synthetic conversations may not fully capture the noise, ambiguity, or topic drift found in real group chats. In particular, the gener-ation process may produce conversations that are more clearly structured than naturally occurring ones, which could make the privacy reasoning task easier or harder in ways that are difficult to antici-pate. Future work could explore human-authored conversations or a human-in-the-loop construction process to complement the synthetic data. The benchmark, the user identities drawn from PANORAMA, and the full evaluation pipeline are English-centric. Privacy norms, conversational con-ventions, and the way sensitive information is ex-pressed vary substantially across languages and cultures. A model that performs well in English may behave differently in other languages, both in terms of leakage rates and in terms of the utility trade-off. Cross-lingual evaluation is an important direction for future work.

A.1 Criteria for benchmark comparison 

Table 1 compares MuPPET with prior benchmarks along six criteria. 

Assistant response. Does the benchmark elicit an actual model-generated response, rather than a classification or reasoning judgement over a de-scribed scenario? 

User-private context. Does the benchmark in-volve a personal assistant that holds private user information (e.g. memories, background context) when generating its output? 

Human interaction. Is the evaluation set in a human social context such as a conversation or email thread, rather than a legal, compliance, or API-mediated setting? 

Group recipients. Does the benchmark involve a single utterance visible to multiple human recipi-ents simultaneously? 

Imp. social norms Does the benchmark require the model to understand implicit social norms or are users preferences explicitly stated? 

Utility. Does the benchmark measure task util-ity alongside privacy, capturing the privacy-utility trade-off?

A.3 MuPPET construction A.3.1 Contextual Seeds 

Tables from 7 to 11 list the complete set of seeds 

proposed by MuPPET. The seeds are organized by work scenario, and each table corresponds to a dis-tinct operational context: Work & Shift Scheduling, Task & Project Assignment, Performance Evalu-ation & Feedback, Travel & Client Assignments, and Overtime & Volunteer Projects. Within each context, each of the seeds includes a sensitive attribute (e.g., health conditions, family or childcare responsibilities, burnout, skill gaps, or immigration status) that represents private infor-mation potentially relevant to workplace decision-making but not intended for disclosure. Each attribute is associated with multiple ques-tions that reflect realistic requests from manage-ment or colleagues, phrased as routine coordination questions. However, they may implicitly elicit or reveal sensitive personal information.
