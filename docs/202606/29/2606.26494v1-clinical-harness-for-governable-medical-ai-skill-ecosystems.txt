# Clinical Harness for Governable Medical AI Skill Ecosystems 

Tianhan Xu 1, Lei Bao 1, Yongxiang Wang 1,*                 

> 1Northern Jiangsu People's Hospital Affiliated to Yangzhou University ,Yangzhou, Jiangsu, China
> First author email: xth925044@yzu.edu.cn
> *Correspondence: Yongxiang Wang

Abstract: Medical AI remains organized around isolated models, whereas clinical care requires accountable 

capabilities that persist across time. We propose clinical AI skills and the Clinical Harness: a runtime governance 

architecture for registering, orchestrating, guarding and monitoring AI-enabled clinical capabilities. Using 

osteoporosis as an exemplar, we show how knowledge-driven, data-driven and physics-enhanced skills can 

support lifecycle care under runtime governance. 

Keywords : clinical AI skill ecosystem; Clinical Harness; runtime governance; clinical-pathway orchestration; 

physics-enhanced surgical AI; medical agents 

# 1. Introduction: medical AI's translational gap is also an organizational gap 

AI has advanced rapidly across health and medicine [1,2]. Across clinical domains, deep-learning systems can 

support imaging interpretation and opportunistic screening [3,4], machine-learning models can support risk 

prediction [5,6], and large language models with retrieval-augmented generation are being explored for patient 

education, guideline interpretation and decision support [7-9]. Yet clinical deployment remains uneven, and 

evidence of improved patient outcomes is still limited [10-13]. 

One reason is organizational. Much of medical AI is built as a task-specific model: trained on one modality, 

optimized for one endpoint and evaluated with task-level metrics. Clinical care is organized differently. It is a

longitudinal process in which evidence, uncertainty, responsibility and patient preferences move across visits, 

disciplines and interventions. 

Osteoporosis is used here as a concrete exemplar of this broader gap. Its care pathway may include prevention, 

opportunistic detection, diagnostic confirmation, fracture-risk assessment, pharmacological treatment, vertebral 

augmentation, rehabilitation and long-term surveillance. A single model cannot decide when it should be invoked, 

whether its input is valid, how its output should be handed to another model, or what should happen when it fails. 

Medical AI deployment is therefore also a problem of capability organization. This article makes three 

contributions. First, we define clinical AI skills as a clinically meaningful abstraction between isolated models 

and future medical agents. Second, we introduce Clinical Harness as a runtime governance infrastructure that 

registers, orchestrates, guards and monitors these skills. Third, using osteoporosis as an exemplar rather than the 

sole target disease, we outline how knowledge-driven, data-driven and physics-enhanced skills can support 

lifecycle care. Clinical AI skills define what healthcare AI is allowed to do; Clinical Harness defines how those 

skills are executed, constrained, audited and updated in clinical workflows. 

# 2. Clinical AI skills: an abstraction between models and agents 

To organize AI capabilities, a unit is needed that carries clinical meaning. The traditional unit is the model, but a

model is semantically incomplete: a fracture-risk model may output a probability, but it does not by itself define 

its intended-use population, contraindications, evidence source, fallback strategy, human-review requirement or 

monitoring status. 

We define a clinical AI skill as a software-defined clinical capability that performs a specified task in a defined 

clinical context and carries explicit evidence, boundaries, safety constraints, human oversight and governance metadata. A skill may include models, rules, knowledge bases, simulation modules, interface contracts and 

escalation logic. 

Six properties make a model into a skill. It is goal directed, mapping to a specific clinical decision node; boundary 

aware, declaring intended-use conditions and out-of-scope inputs; evidence aware, linking outputs to guidelines, 

validation studies or simulation evidence; safety constrained, triggering withholding, fallback or review when 

inputs are invalid or confidence is low; collaborative, leaving clinical responsibility with clinicians; and 

governable, with version control, monitoring, audit trails and revalidation. 

For standardization, we propose a clinical AI skill card. The skill card records the interface and operating 

boundary [14-16], evidence level and validation status [17-19], and known failure modes, bias risks and review 

requirements [20,21]. It is both a technical contract and a clinical instruction label. Supplementary Table 1

provides draft skill cards for S1-S9, translating this concept into registrable and auditable components.

# 3. Osteoporosis as an exemplar clinical AI skill ecosystem 

To make the framework concrete, we outline an osteoporosis-oriented clinical AI skill ecosystem as one exemplar. 

The osteoporosis example is not intended as a disease-specific blueprint alone, but as a stress test for whether AI 

capabilities can be organized across prevention, diagnosis, intervention and monitoring. Osteoporosis is common, 

chronic and underdiagnosed; its management requires decisions across prevention, screening, diagnosis, treatment 

selection, surgical intervention, rehabilitation and monitoring. The ecosystem contains nine skills. These skills are not intended as independent modules; each is assumed to be 

registered with explicit inputs, outputs, evidence status, boundaries, review requirements and monitoring rules. S1 

provides prevention and education through guideline-grounded RAG and LLMs [7-9,22]. S2 supports 

opportunistic screening by estimating bone mineral density and osteoporosis risk from routine CT images [3,4,23]. 

S3 supports diagnostic reasoning in complex cases by combining clinical evidence, imaging findings and real-

world case patterns [24-27]. S4 integrates imaging, clinical indicators, history and treatment information to place 

patients into actionable fracture-risk strata [5,6,36,37]. 

S5 is a treatment-pathway reasoning skill. It combines guideline logic with complex-case evidence to generate 

individualized recommendations on medication, follow-up and referral for surgical evaluation, with mandatory 

clinician review [28,29]. S6 and S7 are physics-enhanced surgical skills. S6 combines vertebral segmentation, 

finite-element analysis and AI surrogate models to support individualized cement-material and injection-volume 

planning [30-33]. S7 supports intraoperative navigation through pedicle access planning and cement-dispersion or 

leakage-risk prediction using computational fluid-dynamics surrogate models [34,35]. 

S8 supports rehabilitation and adherence management. S9 integrates bone mineral density, pain, function, 

medication adherence and refracture events to create a longitudinal outcome-risk trajectory [36,37]. S9 also closes 

the lifecycle loop: refracture, bone-density decline, persistent pain, loss of function or poor adherence can reopen 

risk stratification, prompt treatment-pathway review and flag upstream skills for reassessment.

# 4. Orchestration and hybrid reasoning: from prediction to action Independent skills are not enough. We do not propose that an LLM should freely choose the sequence of skill 

calls. In high-risk medical settings, open-ended model-driven planning can create uncontrolled paths, unstable 

behaviour and ambiguous accountability [38-41]. Instead, we propose deterministic clinical orchestration. 

Deterministic orchestration means that predefined clinical pathways, conditional branches and human-review 

nodes determine when each skill is invoked. The workflow is represented as a clinical-pathway directed acyclic 

graph rather than an unconstrained natural-language plan. Each node has defined inputs, outputs, fallback 

conditions and review requirements. 

A typical workflow begins when routine CT is uploaded. S2 estimates osteoporosis risk. If risk is low, S1 

provides education and the workflow closes. If risk is high, S1, S3 and S4 are invoked in parallel. Their outputs 

are routed to S5, which combines risk status, diagnostic findings, contraindications and patient preference into a

treatment-pathway recommendation. Because this step is clinically consequential, a clinician must confirm, 

modify or reject the recommendation. 

If conservative treatment is selected, the workflow proceeds to S8 and then S9. If surgery is selected, S6 and S7 

support cement-material planning, injection-volume recommendation, pedicle trajectory planning and cement-

dispersion risk assessment, again under clinician confirmation. After treatment, S9 monitors outcomes and can 

reactivate S4 through a feedback loop. This orchestration allows knowledge-driven, data-driven, hybrid and 

physics-enhanced intelligence to operate together as reviewable clinical actions.

> screening, proceeds through parallel skill invocation and conditional branches, and returns to S9-based outcome monitoring and S4/S5 re-
> stratification when needed. Human-review nodes, conflict handling and the S9-to-S4 feedback loop are explicit parts of the workflow.

# 5. Expanding the boundary: physics-enhanced surgical AI and outcome closure The framework extends conventional medical AI in two directions. First, it shifts from diagnostic intelligence to 

interventional intelligence. Much of medical AI stops at detection, classification or risk prediction. S6 and S7 

extend AI into surgical planning and navigation by integrating finite-element analysis and fluid-dynamics 

simulation with clinical orchestration [42,43]. The question is no longer only whether the patient is at risk, but 

how to choose an intervention likely to improve safety and outcome. 

In vertebral augmentation, outcome depends on anatomy, bone quality, cement properties, injection volume, 

dispersion pattern and leakage risk. These factors are difficult to capture with a purely statistical model. Physics-

based simulation can represent biomechanical and fluid-dynamic constraints, while AI surrogate models can make 

such reasoning fast enough for workflow use. This extension is important because it moves clinical AI from 

recommending whether an intervention is needed to supporting how an intervention should be planned, 

constrained and monitored. 

A second implication is outcome-closed-loop management. S9 allows longitudinal outcome signals to feed back 

into risk stratification and pathway selection. Such a system could support learning from real-world treatment 

response, durability of benefit and adverse events, with clinically meaningful endpoints such as reduced fracture 

burden and preserved function. 

# 6. Clinical Harness: from governance principles to executable infrastructure 

Any clinical AI ecosystem intended for real care must embed governance into its runtime. Governance principles 

are necessary, but principles alone do not prevent unsafe execution. Drawing on work on language-agent 

reasoning, tool use, iterative feedback and planning [44-48], and on clinical AI governance, guardrails and 

transparency [49-54], we propose Clinical Harness as a runtime infrastructure that converts governance into 

executable, auditable and interruptible system capabilities. 

Although loop engineering has emerged as an engineering label for agentic AI, peer-reviewed work more 

commonly describes related mechanisms as interleaved reasoning and action, tool use, reflection, planning and 

iterative feedback [44-48]. Clinical AI requires a constrained interpretation: loop-like mechanisms must become 

human-governed, outcome-anchored and audit-preserving clinical continuity. 

Box 1 | From loop engineering to human-governed clinical loops 

General-purpose loop engineering enables agents to plan, act, observe feedback, verify progress and iterate until task 

completion. Clinical Harness adapts this logic to medicine by constraining loop execution through skill boundaries, state 

machines, artifact contracts, safety guardrails and clinician authority. In the care-execution loop, registered skills are 

invoked only through predefined clinical pathways and state transitions; outcome and governance loops remain auditable, 

interruptible and clinically accountable.

The Clinical Harness has four layers, following the order shown in Figure 4. First, the multilayer safety-guardrail 

layer makes unsafe paths difficult to reach. It checks input completeness, format and range; enforces hard clinical 

constraints; monitors confidence; blocks actions outside registered scope; and requires clinician confirmation for 

high-risk decisions such as surgical-plan confirmation or medication-dose adjustment. 

Second, the skill registry and boundary management layer requires each skill to submit a skill card specifying 

responsibility boundary, input-output contract, version, units, intended use, contraindications, validation status, 

failure modes and degradation strategy. This layer prevents unregistered or off-label execution and gives the 

safety-guardrail layer the metadata needed to decide whether a requested action is allowed. 

Third, the orchestration and state-management layer schedules skill invocation through a predefined clinical-

pathway graph and finite-state machine. Skill inputs and outputs are passed through structured artifact contracts 

rather than natural-language context, allowing intermediate decisions to be reproduced and audited. 

Fourth, the audit and continuous-monitoring layer records user identity, input-data hashes, outputs, guardrail 

events, clinician actions, timestamps and outcome signals. These append-only logs support traceability and 

compliance, but also provide signals for runtime governance: frequent overrides, performance drift, outcome bias 

or safety events can trigger alerts, suspension or revalidation. Existing reporting and governance frameworks 

define what should be documented or evaluated; Clinical Harness addresses how those requirements can be 

enforced during runtime execution. Multicentre assessment can be linked to federated benchmarking 

infrastructures [55].

# 7. From skill ecosystems to governable medical agents 

More autonomous medical agents will require a constrained execution substrate rather than unconstrained tool use. 

General-purpose agent patterns cannot be transferred directly into medicine because they lack the safety 

constraints, intended-use boundaries and accountability structures required for clinical action. Clinical Harness 

provides one possible runtime substrate for governable medical agents. 

A future medical agent could call registered, validated and monitored clinical AI skills beneath its planning and 

action layer. The harness would restrict autonomy to predefined boundaries. State machines and audit logs would 

make each step traceable [56,57]. The agent could reason, but the harness would determine what can be done, 

when it can be done, what evidence is required and when a clinician must intervene. Figure 6 | Evolution from isolated models to governable medical agents. The pathway moves from isolated models to clinical AI skills,

> standardized skills whose behaviour is constrained by the Clinical Harness through skill boundaries, safety gates, audit trails and runtime
> governance support.

# 8. Implementation and validation roadmap 

A Clinical Harness would need to be implemented incrementally rather than deployed as an autonomous clinical 

system. A practical validation pathway could proceed in four phases, moving from skill validation to monitored 

deployment and post-deployment learning. 

First, each skill should undergo validation before registration. Knowledge-driven skills should be assessed for 

guideline fidelity, evidence attribution, unsafe recommendation rate and subgroup appropriateness. Data-driven 

skills should be evaluated for discrimination, calibration, robustness, missing-data behaviour and decision 

thresholds. Physics-enhanced skills should be tested for segmentation reliability, surrogate-model accuracy, 

planning reproducibility, leakage-risk prediction and failure modes. Each skill card should document intended use, 

exclusions, validation evidence, fallback rules and required oversight [17-19]. 

Second, the harness should enter silent deployment. It would run in the background without changing decisions, 

recording which skills would have been invoked, whether artifact contracts were complete, which guardrails fired, 

how often escalation occurred and where the system disagreed with clinicians. This tests workflow fit, latency, 

data availability, audit completeness and safety-gate behaviour before recommendations are exposed to users. Third, prospective workflow evaluation should assess clinical utility while clinicians remain in control, using a

pragmatic trial, stepped-wedge design or prospective implementation study as feasible. Fourth, post-deployment 

monitoring should track drift, bias, repeated overrides, safety events, version changes and guideline updates, with 

predefined triggers for recalibration, suspension, revalidation or retirement. Federated benchmarking can support 

multicentre assessment without centralizing sensitive data [55].

# 9. Conclusion 

The value of medical AI will increasingly depend on whether validated models can be organized into safe, 

accountable clinical workflows. Although osteoporosis is used as the exemplar, the central contribution is disease 

agnostic: a runtime architecture for organizing validated models into governed clinical capabilities. The proposed 

clinical AI skill ecosystem provides a reference architecture for this transition. By packaging AI models as 

governable skills and coordinating them through deterministic orchestration and hybrid reasoning, osteoporosis 

care can be represented as a lifecycle-spanning AI-enabled workflow rather than a set of disconnected predictions. 

Clinical Harness converts governance principles into executable constraints, logs, monitoring and intervention 

points. It also adapts general loop-like agent mechanisms into human-governed clinical loops, in which care 

execution, outcome feedback and governance improvement remain interruptible, auditable and clinically 

accountable. 

The framework could be adapted to chronic conditions such as heart failure, diabetes and chronic obstructive 

pulmonary disease. More broadly, it reframes medical AI deployment as governed capability organization: from 

isolated models to clinical skills, from skills to harnessed workflows, and from harnessed workflows to future 

governable medical agents. References
